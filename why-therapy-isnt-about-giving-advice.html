On Psychotherapy

Why Therapy Isn't About Giving Advice

If you wanted someone to tell you what to do, you wouldn't need therapy.

People sometimes come to their first session expecting answers. "Should I leave?" "Should I change jobs?" "Am I overreacting?"

I understand the impulse. When you're in pain, you want someone with expertise to cut through the confusion and tell you what to do.

But that's not what therapy is.

What Advice Does

Advice gives you someone else's answer to your problem. It might be good advice. It might even be right. But it comes from outside — from the advisor's values, experience, and perspective.

The problem with advice in therapy is that it bypasses you. It doesn't help you understand why you're stuck. It doesn't change the pattern that brought you here. It just gives you a next step that may or may not fit.

What Therapy Does Instead

Therapy helps you hear what you already know — but can't yet access. The answer is usually in you. It's just buried under fear, shame, obligation, habit, or the noise of other people's expectations.

My job isn't to know what's right for you. My job is to create the conditions in which you can know it for yourself.

That means staying with confusion rather than resolving it prematurely. Sitting with discomfort rather than soothing it away. Trusting that if we slow down enough, clarity will come.

This Is Harder Than It Sounds

It's harder than getting advice. Advice is quick. Therapy takes time. Advice lets you outsource the decision. Therapy asks you to own it.

But the change that comes from therapy — because it comes from inside — tends to hold. It's not borrowed confidence. It's yours.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
