On Depression

Depression as Exhaustion: When Anxiety Has Been Running Too Long

Sometimes depression isn't the beginning of the problem. It's the body's way of collapsing after years of alarm.

Many people come to therapy saying they're depressed. They feel flat, unmotivated, disconnected. They've lost interest in things that used to matter. They sleep too much or not at all.

When we slow down and trace the timeline, a pattern often emerges: the depression didn't come first. Anxiety did.

The Collapse After the Storm

Anxiety is the nervous system in overdrive — scanning for danger, bracing for the worst, running on adrenaline. This is sustainable for a while. Months. Sometimes years.

But the body has limits. When the system has been in fight-or-flight for too long, it doesn't gradually calm down. It crashes. The crash looks like depression — flatness, withdrawal, numbness, loss of motivation.

This isn't laziness. It's not a character flaw. It's a nervous system that has exhausted its resources.

Why This Matters for Treatment

If we treat the depression without understanding the anxiety beneath it, we're treating the symptom, not the source. The person may feel slightly better — but the underlying pattern remains, and the cycle repeats.

In therapy, we work with both. We attend to the flatness and disconnection. And we trace it back to the chronic vigilance that preceded it — the relationships, environments, and early experiences that taught the nervous system to stay on alert.

What Recovery Looks Like

It's not a sudden lift. It's gradual. Energy returns in small waves. Interest flickers back. The body begins to trust that it's safe enough to feel again.

Therapy supports this by providing a steady, reliable relationship — a place where the nervous system can slowly recalibrate. Not through techniques alone, but through the experience of being with someone who is present, consistent, and unhurried.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
