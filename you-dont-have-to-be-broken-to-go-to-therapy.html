Starting Therapy

You Don't Have to Be 'Broken' to Go to Therapy

Therapy isn't a treatment for brokenness. It's a space for self-understanding.

There's a persistent idea — more felt than spoken — that therapy is for people who are broken. Who can't cope. Who have something seriously wrong with them.

In Singapore, this idea carries additional cultural weight. Seeking help can feel like an admission of weakness, a loss of face, a failure to manage what others seem to manage just fine.

This keeps many people away from therapy until they're in crisis. And it means they carry, silently and alone, burdens that could have been shared much earlier.

The Stigma

Singapore has made progress in mental health awareness. The conversations are more open than they were a decade ago. But stigma doesn't disappear with awareness campaigns. It lives in the small moments: the hesitation before telling a friend you're seeing a therapist. The worry about what your employer might think. The voice in your head that says other people have real problems — I should just be tougher.

In many Asian cultures, there's an additional layer: the expectation that personal difficulties should be handled within the family, or simply endured. Seeking outside help — particularly for emotional issues — can feel like breaking an unspoken rule.

I grew up in this culture. I understand it from the inside. I also know that the rule was never designed to protect the person carrying the weight. It was designed to protect the family's appearance of having no weight to carry.

What Therapy Actually Is

Therapy isn't a treatment for brokenness. It's a space for self-understanding.

People come to therapy for all kinds of reasons. Yes, some come because they're in acute distress. But many come because they want to understand themselves better. Because they've noticed a pattern they want to change. Because they're navigating a difficult transition. Because they have an audacious aspiration and want to be clearer about what's getting in the way.

Most of my clients continue having sessions not because they're in crisis but because they've found that therapy is a good resource for finding their ground. They use their sessions as what one client called "an internal reset button."

What Strength Actually Looks Like

Walking into a therapy room and saying "I need help" isn't a sign of weakness. It's one of the braver things a person can do — particularly in a context where the cultural expectation is to handle things quietly and alone.

Strength isn't the absence of struggle. It's the willingness to face what's difficult rather than avoid it.

Starting

If you've been thinking about therapy but haven't taken the step — if you've been telling yourself you're not "bad enough" to need it — consider the possibility that you don't need to be bad at all.

You just need to be curious about what might change if you gave yourself permission to explore.

Come as you are here — exactly as you are.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
