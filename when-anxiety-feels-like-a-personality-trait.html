Anxiety

When Anxiety Feels Like a Personality Trait

It isn't who you are. It's what you learned to carry.

There's a moment — and it usually happens quietly, over years — when anxiety stops feeling like something that visits you and starts feeling like something you are.

You stop saying "I feel anxious" and start saying "I'm an anxious person."

The symptom becomes the identity.

By the time people arrive in my consulting room with this experience, anxiety has been their companion for so long that imagining life without it feels impossible. Or naive. Or frightening in a different way — because if you take away the vigilance, what's left?

How Anxiety Becomes Identity

When anxiety begins in childhood — which it often does — it weaves itself into the developing self. The child who learns to scan every room for danger grows into the adult who "just has a careful nature." The teenager who couldn't sleep before exams becomes the professional who "thrives under pressure" while quietly burning out.

The over-preparation. The people-pleasing. The need to control. The difficulty saying no.

These aren't personality traits. They're adaptive responses — creative adjustments, in Gestalt terms — to a nervous system that learned early on to stay on alert. The child needed these strategies to survive. The adult is still running them, long after the original danger has passed.

The Cost of Normalising It

When anxiety becomes "just who you are," it becomes invisible. You stop questioning it. You build your entire life around accommodating it — choosing careers, relationships, and routines that keep the anxiety manageable rather than ones that genuinely suit you.

I see this regularly. A life shaped by avoidance rather than desire. A persistent tiredness that no amount of rest resolves. A feeling of performing a role rather than living a life.

The body keeps score. Insomnia. Skin flares. Digestive issues. Weakened immunity. Tension that never fully resolves. Even when we ignore our distress, our bodies don't.

What I Work With

The work isn't about eliminating anxiety. That would be neither possible nor desirable — anxiety, in its healthy form, is a signal. It tells us something matters.

What I work with is the separation between you and it. In Gestalt therapy, we pay attention to the present moment: what's happening in your body right now, what tightens when you speak about certain things, what relaxes when you're given permission to be honest.

Awareness itself can be regulating.

Slowly, the anxiety starts to move from the foreground to the background. Not gone — but no longer running the show. You begin to notice: there is a self underneath the vigilance. And that self has preferences, desires, and capacities that anxiety has been obscuring for a long time.

What Clients Notice

It's rarely dramatic. Someone realises they made a decision without agonising over it. They notice they slept through the night. They catch themselves laughing without the usual undercurrent of dread.

These moments aren't the result of a technique. They emerge from a gradual process of reclaiming what anxiety took — bit by bit, session by session, until the life you're living starts to feel more like your own.

You are not your anxiety. You are the person who learned to carry it.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
