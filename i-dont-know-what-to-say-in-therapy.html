Starting Therapy

I Don't Know What to Say in Therapy

You don't need a script. You don't need to be ready. You just need to show up.

This is the most common thing people tell me before their first session.

Sometimes they say it apologetically, as though arriving without a prepared speech is some kind of failure. Sometimes they say it in the waiting room, almost as a warning — I don't know where to start. As if I might send them away for not having done their homework.

I won't.

What the Room Actually Looks Like

A session feels like a conversation in a living room — unhurried, natural, grounded. Sometimes Mulan, my Singapore Special dog, is there too, quietly present. There is no clipboard. No diagnostic checklist waiting at the door. No correct way to begin.

We sit. We start wherever you are.

Maybe that's a specific problem — a relationship falling apart, a job that's draining you, a panic attack that came out of nowhere. Maybe it's vaguer — a sense that something isn't working, a flatness you can't explain, a restlessness that won't settle. Maybe it's silence for the first few minutes while you figure out what to say.

All of that is welcome here.

You Don't Need to Have Your Thoughts Organised

I have sat with people who arrived with a clear story and people who arrived with nothing but a feeling they couldn't name. Both are equally valid starting points.

Part of the work is finding language for experiences that don't yet have words. If you could already articulate everything perfectly, you probably wouldn't need to be here. The not-knowing is not the obstacle. It's the material.

What If I Say the Wrong Thing?

There is no wrong thing.

If you're anxious about being in therapy, that anxiety is something we can work with. If you're sceptical about whether it will help, that scepticism is welcome. If you spend the first ten minutes talking about the weather because you can't get to the thing underneath it — that's fine. The thing underneath it will show up when it's ready.

The therapeutic relationship is built on honesty, not performance. You don't need to be articulate, insightful, or composed. You just need to be present.

What the First Session Is Really About

For most people, the work begins in the first session — not after some preliminary assessment phase, but right there, in the room. I'm already paying attention to what matters: how you describe your world, what you notice, what you avoid, how your body responds when you touch on certain topics.

The breath that catches mid-sentence. The hand that goes to the throat. The moment the rehearsed story breaks and something real enters the room.

Simply being heard — without interruption, without advice, without judgement — is already something different from what most people are used to. That difference is therapeutic in itself.

On Readiness

If you're reading this and wondering whether you're "ready" for therapy, consider that the wondering itself is a form of readiness.

You don't need to wait until things are unbearable. You don't need to have exhausted every other option. You don't need to arrive knowing what to say.

You just need to show up. The rest unfolds from there.

Therapy isn't something that happens to you here. It unfolds with you.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
