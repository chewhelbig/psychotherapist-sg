On Relationships

What It Means to Be Present in a Relationship

Connection doesn't require perfection. It requires presence.

We talk about presence as if it were simple. "Be present." "Stay in the moment." As though all you need to do is put your phone down and make eye contact.

Real presence is harder than that. And rarer.

What Presence Actually Is

Presence means being available — emotionally, physically, attentionally — to what is happening right now between you and another person. Not planning your response. Not defending against what you're about to hear. Not performing interest while your mind is elsewhere.

It means staying when it gets uncomfortable. Noticing without judging. Responding rather than reacting.

Why It's So Hard

Because presence makes us vulnerable. When you're fully here, you can be hurt. You can be seen. You might feel something you'd rather avoid.

Most of us have learned, very early, that it's safer to be slightly absent. To numb, distract, intellectualise, withdraw. These are not failures. They're adaptations — creative adjustments that once protected us.

But in intimate relationships, they become the very thing that erodes connection.

Presence in Couples Work

In my practice, some of the most powerful moments happen when one partner simply turns toward the other and says what they're actually feeling — without defensiveness, without performance, without strategy.

That's presence. And it changes everything.

Gestalt therapy cultivates this. Not through exercises or scripts, but through the lived experience of being genuinely met — first by the therapist, and then, gradually, by each other.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
