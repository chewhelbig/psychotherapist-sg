Gestalt Therapy · Philosophical Ground

What Makes Gestalt Therapy Gestalt:
My Philosophical Ground

The difference is philosophical before it is technical. It lives in where the therapist places their attention, what they trust, and what they are willing to not know.

People sometimes ask what makes Gestalt therapy different from other approaches. The honest answer is that the difference is philosophical before it is technical. It lives in where the therapist places their attention, what they trust, and what they are willing to not know.

I wrote this piece because clarity matters — both for people considering therapy and for fellow practitioners navigating a landscape crowded with overlapping vocabularies. What follows is my attempt to articulate the philosophical ground I stand on, and to trace — honestly — how that ground relates to seven other major therapeutic approaches.

Three Commitments

Phenomenology is the discipline of attending to what presents itself before we explain it away. When a client tells me he is “in a bad place,” I notice it. I write it down. I let the session go on. I resist the pull to classify the statement. I notice instead the quality of his voice, the metaphor he has chosen, the atmosphere that gathers around those words. It may be months before either of us understands what “that place” actually is. My job is to stay close to the figure without forcing it to declare itself prematurely. This is what Staemmler (2009) calls the willingness to be uncertain. It is uncomfortable. It is also where the real work lives.

Field theory means the person is never understood in isolation. Organism and environment are a single reality. This sounds abstract until you sit in a room with a couple and feel the invisible architecture of their relationship pressing on your chest. Francesetti (2015) has taken this further: suffering does not reside inside the patient. It arises between persons, in the lived situation of their encounter. When I feel dread reading a client’s intake documents, that dread is data — field data, co-created in the space between what I know and what I am about to encounter.

Creative indifference — Friedlaender’s (1918) Schöpferische Indifferenz — is widely misunderstood. It is not neutrality. It is a dynamic stance at the centre between polarities, holding both without collapsing into either. When I sit with a couple on the verge of divorce, one begging to stay, the other already gone — I do not take sides. I hold the whole of what is presenting itself and trust that something will emerge from the centre. Beisser’s (1970) paradoxical theory of change is the clinical expression: transformation occurs through the full acceptance of what is, not through forcing the organism to be different.

In Dialogue With Seven Approaches

How Gestalt Therapy Relates to Other Modalities

These three commitments mentioned are an overview of the deep philosophical roots of Gestalt therapy, producing a therapeutic stance that both converges with and diverges from the major contemporary modalities. My stance is clear: I see Gestalt therapy as most complete and efficacious of all therapeutic teachings. That's my honest assessment. For if that weren't the case, wouldn't I be practicing something the other modalities instead? Here is a tracing of those intersections.

Internal Family Systems (IFS)

IFS and Gestalt therapy both recognise that human experience is polyphonic. The question is what we do with that recognition. IFS reifies multiplicity into literal sub-personalities — protectors, managers, firefighters, exiles. Gestalt therapy sees the same phenomena as fixed Gestalts: rigid patterns of creative adjustment that once served survival but have become frozen. The question shifts from “What does this part want?” to “What field conditions call forth this pattern?”

The Gestalt understanding of self is also different. There is no core, undamaged essence waiting to be liberated. Self is a process — a system of contacts, flexible and always in formation. Therapeutic change depends on the client’s capacity for fresh contact with what is actually here, not on accessing a buried essence.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

CBT begins with cognition. Its premise is that distress arises from distorted thinking that can be identified and corrected. The distance from Gestalt therapy is vast. When a client says “I am worthless,” a CBT therapist might identify a core belief to challenge. I stay with the phenomenon. How does worthlessness live in this person’s body right now? What atmosphere does it create between us? I do not presume to know better than the client what their experience means.

The convergence has grown, though. Mindfulness-based CBT has moved closer to the phenomenological attitude — inviting clients to observe their thoughts without judgment, approaching through the back door what has always been Gestalt therapy’s front entrance.

Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT)

DBT’s central dialectic — the tension between acceptance and change — mirrors the paradoxical theory of change. The resonance is genuine. The divergence is in the mechanism: DBT teaches structured coping skills prescribed from outside the client’s experience. Gestalt therapy trusts that when the organism is fully aware of its present situation, self-regulation emerges from within. The change is discovered, not learned.

DBT’s clinical populations — individuals with borderline features, severe dysregulation — are precisely those who most need relational holding. The risk of a skills-based approach is locating the problem in the client’s deficient self-regulation rather than in the field conditions that made self-regulation so difficult.

EMDR

EMDR proposes that traumatic memories are stored in unprocessed form and that bilateral stimulation facilitates their integration. The notion that trauma remains unfinished resonates with the Gestalt concept of the open Gestalt. Where the two approaches part: EMDR locates healing in the brain’s processing system. Gestalt therapy locates it in the relational field — in the lived encounter between a client and a therapist who is genuinely present and genuinely willing to be disturbed.

Both agree trauma must be contacted, not merely discussed. The divergence is about where that contact takes place.

Somatic Therapy

Somatic approaches share with Gestalt therapy a commitment to the body. Both reject the Cartesian split. The alignment is deep. The distinction is subtle but consequential: many somatic approaches treat the body as a site of stored trauma to be discharged or regulated through specific techniques. Gestalt therapy’s relationship to the body is more radical. The body is the ground from which figure emerges. It is trusted as the organism’s means of creative contact with its environment, not as a mechanism to be regulated.

In my clinical experience, the most transformative moments arise not from techniques applied to the body but from atmospheric shifts that occur when genuine contact is made.

Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)

EFT, grounded in attachment theory, may have the most natural affinity with Gestalt therapy’s relational emphasis. In my couples work (Chew-Helbig, 2026), I have explored how attachment patterns manifest as field phenomena — a tightening grip, a gradual withdrawal, the atmospheric quality of abandonment filling the room.

The divergence: EFT pursues secure attachment as a therapeutic goal. Gestalt therapy pursues no predetermined state. It attends to the phenomenon of the moment and attunes to the pathos of the field. Jaspers (1913/1997) understood this: psychopathology is the study of the soul’s suffering, not the diagnosis of mental illness.

Psychoanalysis

The oldest conversation here. Perls trained as an analyst; Gestalt therapy emerged partly as creative rebellion against psychoanalysis’s excessive intellectualism. Yet the family resemblance runs deeper than either tradition acknowledges — Freud’s structural model, Klein’s object relations, Winnicott’s transitional space.

The divergence is temporal. Psychoanalysis works through the past. Gestalt therapy works in the present: whatever the history, it is available only as it organises current experience. Contemporary relational psychoanalysis has moved closer — the Boston Change Process Study Group’s (2010) “moments of meeting” describe something remarkably similar to what Gestalt has always called contact.

Historical Context

A Timeline — And What It Reveals

Psychoanalysis stands at the beginning. Freud published The Interpretation of Dreams in 1900. Everything else follows from — or reacts to — that original conversation about the unconscious, the body, and human suffering.

Year Modality Founder(s) Trained In
1900 Psychoanalysis Sigmund Freud (1900) Neurology
1951 Gestalt Therapy Fritz Perls, Laura Perls, Paul Goodman (1951) Psychoanalysis
1960s CBT Aaron Beck (1967) Psychoanalysis
~1985 IFS Richard Schwartz (1995) Systemic family therapy
~1985 EFT Sue Johnson (1985) Humanistic & systemic therapy
1987 EMDR Francine Shapiro (1989) English literature & behavioural therapy
Late 1980s Somatic Experiencing Peter Levine (1997) Biophysics & psychology
1993 DBT Marsha Linehan (1993) Behavioural psychology

Gestalt therapy was not a rejection of psychoanalysis. It was its completion — an attempt to restore the body, the present moment, and the relational field that classical analysis had intellectualised away.

CBT moved in a fundamentally different direction. Beck left the consulting room’s depth for the laboratory’s measurability. CBT is not psychotherapy in the relational sense. It is a behavioural intervention with a cognitive frame. Effective for what it targets. But it does not sit in the same philosophical lineage.

Then came the branded modalities — five of them within a single decade, all by clinicians trained in earlier traditions, all repackaging elements that already existed in humanistic, existential, and body-based approaches. Writing this, we might question the relevance of focussing on these techniques alone in today's post pandemic, multicultural world, where Artificial Intelligence is part of our psychological ecosystem.

This is not a dismissal. Each of these modalities has contributed clinical innovations in the past, and reflect primarily Western cultures. DBT’s work with severe dysregulation is genuinely important. EMDR has pin-pointed the use of physical stimulation as part of the process while facilitating access of traumatic material. EFT brought attachment theory into the couples room, a re-iteration of what psychoanalysis had already brought to light through the concept of transference.

But it is worth noticing the pattern. A clinician encounters a limitation in adopting the modality they were trained in. They discover something — often something that phenomenological and humanistic therapies have practised for decades — and build a branded protocol around it. Many also recognise a marketing opportunity. In the United States more than elsewhere, rebranding is a thing, a way of colonialisation. The protocol acquires a training institute, a certification process, a trademark. The insight becomes a product. Branding is commercial, and it tends to carry an implicit message: that this particular way of working is superior to others.

The net result is no enhancement to the mental profession, but a dumbing down of what psychotherapists already have, a rich philosophical inheritance from psychoanalysis.

That message is not supported by the evidence. Over thirty years of psychotherapy research — most notably the work of Bruce Wampold and the common factors literature — has consistently shown that therapeutic outcomes are not modality-specific. They are therapist-specific. What determines whether therapy works is the quality of the therapeutic relationship, the skill and presence of the therapist, and the client’s active participation — not the brand name on the door (Wampold, 2001, 2015; Wampold & Imel, 2015; Norcross & Lambert, 2018). Some modalities invest heavily in funding research to demonstrate their superiority. But when bona fide therapies are compared head to head, the differences between them are negligible. The differences between therapists are not.

Psychotherapy is psychotherapy. The rest is marketing.

The Training Industry

Why So Many Modalities? Because Courses Sell

So why do we have so many modalities and certification programmes today? Because courses sell. The training industry plays on the insecurity of counsellors — the quiet worry that you are not enough, that you need one more certificate, one more technique, one more acronym after your name. The more a programme promises answers, the more it matches that insecurity, the more money it makes. Each course and certification costs thousands of dollars. The industry that has grown around branded modalities is enormous — and its primary customer is not the client. It is the therapist.

My own experience tells a different story. My biggest learning has not come from training programmes. It has come from the work itself — from sitting with clients, hour after hour, year after year, and paying attention to what actually happens in the room. That is where clinical knowledge lives. Not in a manual. Not in a weekend workshop. In the accumulated weight of thousands of sessions with real people whose lives are not reducible to protocols.

There is something else the branded modalities cannot teach: culture. Culture changes dynamically — it shifts beneath your feet while you are sitting with someone. The person across from you carries a cultural world that no certification programme can fully prepare you for. Understanding how shame operates in a Singaporean Chinese family, how silence functions between a Japanese couple, how migration reshapes identity — this is knowledge that matters more than technique. And it is only learnable through sustained, attentive clinical work with the people who walk through your door.

At a Glance

Five Questions Across Eight Modalities

Five questions, seven modalities. The Gestalt column holds steady throughout.

What is the self?

Gestalt: A process — not a fixed thing but a living system of contacts that forms between people. It is situational, flexible, and always in motion.

IFS: A core essence — the “Self” is inherently compassionate, curious, calm; separate from parts.

CBT: A cognitive agent with beliefs, schemas, and automatic thoughts.

DBT: An individual with skills deficits requiring psychoeducation.

EMDR: A neurological system with adaptive and maladaptive memory networks.

Somatic: An organism with a nervous system that stores and processes traumatic experience.

EFT: Shaped by early attachment; organised around internal working models.

Psychoanalysis: Structured by unconscious forces (classical); relational configurations (contemporary).

Where does suffering live?

Gestalt: In the field — between persons, in the atmosphere of the shared situation.

IFS: In exiled parts — wounded inner personalities carrying burdens from the past.

CBT: In distorted cognitions that misrepresent reality.

DBT: In insufficient capacity to regulate emotions and tolerate distress.

EMDR: In unprocessed traumatic memories stored dysfunctionally in neural networks.

Somatic: In the body — as stored activation, frozen survival responses, autonomic dysregulation.

EFT: In insecure attachment patterns established in childhood.

Psychoanalysis: In unconscious conflict, repressed material, and early relational templates.

How does change happen?

Gestalt: Through fresh contact with what is actually here — in the body, in the room, between people. When awareness is restored, the organism self-regulates. The person does not need to be told what to do. They begin to move toward what they need.

IFS: Through Self-leadership negotiating with, witnessing, and unburdening parts.

CBT: Through identification and replacement of cognitive distortions.

DBT: Through balancing acceptance and change; acquiring structured coping skills.

EMDR: Through bilateral stimulation activating adaptive information processing.

Somatic: Through titration, pendulation, and discharge of stored activation.

EFT: Through accessing primary emotions; restructuring attachment bonds.

Psychoanalysis: Through insight (classical); through new relational knowing (contemporary).

What is the therapist’s position?

Gestalt: Phenomenological witness — present in the client’s life-space, not outside it. The therapist is shaped by the field and shapes it. Observation and participation are not separate acts.

IFS: Facilitator of inner dialogue between the client’s Self and their parts.

CBT: Expert diagnostician of faulty thinking; guides restructuring.

DBT: Teacher and coach; validates while pushing for behavioural change.

EMDR: Technician guiding a neurological process; monitors and facilitates.

Somatic: Skilled tracker of the client’s nervous system; guides somatic interventions.

EFT: Attachment-informed guide; creates safety to access vulnerable emotions.

Psychoanalysis: Interpreter of unconscious material (classical); acknowledges countertransference (contemporary).

What is the therapeutic goal?

Gestalt: Awareness — brought about through contact and dialogue. Like lighting up a life-space that was previously in darkness. When the client can see clearly, they know where to go. The goal may change. The capacity to see is permanent.

IFS: Self-leadership — the core Self governing the internal system with compassion.

CBT: Rational thinking; symptom reduction through cognitive correction.

DBT: A “life worth living” through emotional regulation and interpersonal effectiveness.

EMDR: Desensitisation and adaptive reprocessing of traumatic material.

Somatic: Regulation of the autonomic nervous system; completing thwarted survival responses.

EFT: Secure attachment; emotional bonding through expression of unmet needs.

Psychoanalysis: Making the unconscious conscious (classical); expanded capacity for relating (contemporary).

The Whole

The Wholeness That Cannot Be Split

After years of practice and research, I have come to understand that Gestalt therapy is distinguished less by what it does than by what it refuses to do. It refuses to split the person into parts. It refuses to locate pathology inside an individual abstracted from their field. It refuses to privilege the therapist’s knowledge over the organism’s wisdom.

Each modality I have described offers genuine contributions. CBT’s rigour. EFT’s relational depth. DBT’s pragmatism with severe distress. Somatic therapy’s fidelity to the body. Psychoanalysis’s willingness to sit with complexity. Gestalt therapy can draw from all of these — but holds them lightly, with creative indifference, without collapsing into any single framework.

Gestalt therapy has no trademark. No single founder who controlled its dissemination. No certification empire. It is a philosophical orientation that has been practised, argued about, and refined by a community for over seventy years. That is a different kind of thing.

What I offer in my practice is not a brand. It is a way of being with another person — informed by philosophy, shaped by thousands of hours of clinical work, and continually refined by the cultural realities of the people I sit with. The map is not the territory. The living moment between two people — with all its pathos and creative potential — remains more profound than any theory can capture.

My work begins and ends in that moment. Everything else is commentary.
