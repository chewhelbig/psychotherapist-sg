Why I'm Moving My Psychotherapy Practice to Tiong Bahru

Nicole Chew-Helbig, PhD · Integrative Gestalt Psychotherapist, Singapore

After several years on the river, my psychotherapy practice is moving to Tiong Bahru. From 1 July 2026, sessions will take place at 65 Tiong Poh Road, #02-26 — a second-floor walk-up in the conservation quarter, two minutes from Tiong Bahru Market.

This is not a relocation of convenience. It is a settling.

A practice has a body. It needs ground.

For the last several years my consulting room sat at The Riverwalk — a small mall by the water, near Clarke Quay MRT. The address was clean. Clients found me easily. The location did its job.

But a mall is not ground. A mall is foot traffic and air-conditioning, retail tenancies and shuttered shopfronts. A psychotherapy practice inside a mall borrows its setting from commerce. The work happens in spite of the building, not within it.

Tiong Bahru is also central. It is also easy to find. But it is also a neighbourhood — Singapore's first public housing estate, built in the 1930s, low-rise, lived-in, conserved. The streets have trees. People live above the shops and walk to coffee. The light is good.

The building stops being a thing to tolerate. It participates.

The room itself

The new consulting room is on the second floor of a walk-up shophouse. Three windows. Wooden shutters. A space that was lived in before it was a workspace.

After years of working in a windowless mall unit, this matters. You can feel the difference between a room with daylight and a room without it within the first ten minutes of a session. Bodies regulate to the light they are sitting in.

The room is also larger than the Riverwalk one — enough space for couples work without anyone feeling cornered, enough space for the slow movement that real therapeutic work asks of a body.

For clients who find stairs difficult, I keep a second consulting room at street level on the same lane. Same neighbourhood, different access. No-one is excluded by the geometry of the building.

Practical, and more than practical

Parking is straightforward in the estate. Tiong Bahru MRT is a short walk. The market and food centre are next door. Traffic into the estate, even at peak hour, is gentle compared to what circles the river.

The neighbourhood is also safe and anonymous in a way a mall location can never be. There are no glass shopfronts onto a corridor of strangers. There is no foot traffic past the door. The lane is residential. You arrive without being seen by anyone who isn't already minding their own business.

That kind of privacy is a clinical good, not just a comfort.

Less clinical, more human

For the past few years I have been steadily moving my practice toward something more humanistic and less clinical. Less hospital. Less office. Less service-provider efficiency. More room. More texture. More of what life actually feels like.

This shift matters more now than it did three years ago.

We are in the middle of a profound change in how human beings receive emotional support. AI has entered the relational field as a third organism — competent, available, infinitely patient, never tired, never hungry. Millions of people now turn to it at midnight when something hurts. I have written about this elsewhere on the blog. I am not opposed to it. I am genuinely curious about it.

But the existence of a competent AI listener on every phone makes a question newly urgent: what is human therapy actually for?

It is not for information. It is not for technique. It is not for scripts. The chatbot can do those — sometimes better than a person can.

What we need now is not more counsellors. What we need is deep psychotherapeutic work — the work that demands a human nervous system in the room, a face that does not look away, a body that could leave but chooses to stay.

In my essay Shame as the Last Human Frontier, I argue that shame — the deepest and most isolating affect we know — cannot be metabolised by an AI no matter how sophisticated. Shame requires another human consciousness, another set of fallible eyes, to be transformed. Almost everything else can now be talked about with a chatbot. Shame, finally, cannot.

This is the work my practice is increasingly built around. The setting needs to match it.

A second-floor room in a 1930s shophouse is not a clinical space. It does not signal hospital, or office, or efficient service-delivery. It signals: someone lives here. This is the texture of human life. We will sit in it together.

Same couch, same dog, same hours

What is not changing: the work, the fees, the hours, Mulan. The two armchairs. The bookshelf. The pace. The first session begins where you are.

I'll be at Riverwalk through the end of June 2026. The Tiong Bahru room opens 1 July 2026.
