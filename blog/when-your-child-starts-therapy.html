For Parents

When Your Child Starts Therapy — and You Feel Blamed

By Nicole Chew-Helbig, PhD · Gestalt Psychotherapist, Singapore

Your child is seeing a therapist. You arranged it, you drive them there, you pay for it — and every week, sitting outside that room, a small voice asks: what are they saying about me in there?

Almost every parent feels this. The doubt — about yourself, and about the therapy — arrives uninvited: if my child needs therapy, have I missed something? What went wrong? Sometimes a well-meaning professional makes it worse with a questionnaire about the home environment, and suddenly you feel less like a parent and more like a suspect.

Let me say something plainly. A child needing help is not a verdict on your parenting. Children struggle for a hundred reasons — temperament, school, friendships, loss, a world that moves too fast for a developing nervous system. And yes, sometimes family patterns play a part, because families are a child's growing environment. I will ask a lot about the home, because the information is useful — if not essential — in therapeutic work.

I do not seek to diagnose faults. I look for patterns — patterns in behaviour and in relationship dynamics. Sometimes this investigating process can seem like judgement, and I will continually check with you if you feel that way. I will then offer my hypothesis — what I think is likely happening — and we will experiment with tiny steps of change.

A child does their best work in therapy when home does not require them to manage a parent's anxiety about the therapy. If they come out of a session quiet, let them be quiet for a while.

And here is the harder, more generous truth: the best thing you can do for a child in therapy is often to tend to yourself. I share this view with my colleagues. Your own session, your own room, where your fear and guilt and grief about your child can be spoken without landing on them. Parents who do this free the child from fixed relationship dynamics and facilitate change through the family system. The whole system breathes. In my therapy with parents, I provide psychoeducation and parent coaching while working on your personal stressors.

You did the hard, loving thing by getting them help. You are allowed the same.
