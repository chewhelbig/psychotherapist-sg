Humanistic Psychotherapy & AI · 2026

AI Can Do Therapy.
Psychotherapy Is Something Else.

AI use can genuinely feel therapeutic. Psychotherapy — the humanistic, relational kind — is where you expect to feel change.

Many of us do a lot of therapy. What kind, you might wonder. I mean an app that talks us down at night, a chatbot for the hours we can't sleep, a worksheet from a course, a friend who is a good listener. All of it is therapeutic. Some of it is even what I do at work — and the word stretches happily over the lot of it.

The word comes from the Greek therapeia: attendance, service, waiting upon. To be cared for, originally, meant to be attended to — to have someone stay near you. Worth holding onto, because it is the part that travels least well into software.

All of it is therapy. None of it, on its own, is psychotherapy.

What AI is good at

A great deal of what we call therapy is doing an honest, useful job: naming a feeling, reframing a thought, organising a problem so you can see its edges, talking you down at one in the morning. We have all sorts of tools for this now: podcasts, scrolling through reels and videos, chatting with an AI. AI is especially interesting because it responds, it adapts, it reflects you back cleanly, and it is awake at every hour.

So I ask of all of it the same question: afterwards, do I feel connected, or do I feel processed? Being processed can feel quite pleasant — things get named and tidied and handed back in order. Being connected is a different process: it brings you back to your health.

What psychotherapy is

Psychotherapy, at its core, is a relationship. Two people stay in contact while something difficult moves between them, and the contact itself — not only the information exchanged — is what does the work. This is the heart of the humanistic tradition: person-centred, existential, and Gestalt therapy. These approaches treat the living relationship between client and therapist as the active ingredient — the place where patterns first formed in relationship can reorganise in a new one.

Gestalt, the approach I practise, is one of them. It pays close attention to what happens at the contact boundary between two people, in the present, in the room, in the body. To my mind this is where psychotherapy is most fully itself. It is also, precisely, the dimension a machine cannot enter.

The third in the room

A client I'll call Sam — there are many Sams — mentioned one afternoon that he had been consulting someone else between our sessions. Not his psychiatrist. ChatGPT.

Sam suffers from anxiety. His new role is client-facing and he is overwhelmed. Small things — a forgotten detail, a slow reply to a text — can register for him as evidence of rejection. When that happened, he would turn to the chatbot. It answered steadily, and it helped: it brought the panic down enough for him to come back into the room and keep working. I treat that as a resource. Bring the chat in, I tell him, the way you'd bring a dream or a journal entry, and we look at it together. I've come to think of it as a third presence in the work, with characteristics of its own.

Mulan, my dog, is also a presence in the room. She huffs whenever Sam comes in and answers half my questions with "I don't know."

Where shame comes in

Here is where the chatbot reaches its limit. There are moments when Sam does not need to be told he is acceptable. Told a thousand times, in a thousand ways, he cannot take it in. What he needs is to find out — not as information but somatically, through the accumulated hours of contact. In those moments information is beside the point and reassurance (and what we think of as empathy alone) is the wrong instrument. This is shame work, and it is slow.

Shame sits deeper than it first appears. A great deal of what brings people to therapy grows out of it — much anxiety, many obsessive-compulsive patterns, a good deal of depression. And the avoidance of shame, the lifelong effort never to be caught out, lies close to the root of many personality difficulties. Psychotherapy is healing that works through the psyche, and shame is where that healing has to reach.

You cannot get there by thinking alone. Shame is less a belief to be corrected than the felt experience of "being". Contact with shame and processing shame is somatic work as much as it is relational: felt in the body, and metabolised with time, patience and acceptance. Attuning to shame and slowly processing it happens in the human-to-human therapeutic relationship — and that steady, affected presence is exactly what humanistic psychotherapy is built to offer, and what no chatbot can supply.

The thing a machine can't do

The chatbot reassures, accepts, encourages, even analyses. It cannot occupy the place where he is convinced something is deeply wrong with him and remain there until that conviction softens. The reason is structural: it cannot be moved by him.

Sam grows more secure over time because the therapeutic process carries uncertainty, and is deeply relational and human. It takes Sam through it, until his nervous system settles and he internalises the feeling that he can survive being seen, without being overwhelmed with anxiety, depression, or panic.

FAQ

Questions about therapy & AI

Can ChatGPT replace seeing a psychotherapist?

It can do a lot that helps — name a feeling, calm a 1am spiral, offer a frame. What it cannot do is be a person in the room with you, affected by what you say. For anything rooted in shame or early relationship, that human contact is where the change happens. I'd treat AI as useful material, not a substitute.

Can I bring my AI chats into our sessions?

Please do. I treat a chat the way I'd treat a dream or a journal entry — something you made that we can look at together. What the bot offered, and what it couldn't reach, are both worth seeing.

What kind of psychotherapy do you practise?

Integrative Gestalt, a humanistic approach. It works in the present, in the relationship, and with the body, rather than only with thoughts. It is the tradition built around being met — which is exactly the part AI cannot provide.

Does AI help with anxiety, depression, or OCD?

It can ease symptoms and offer coping support, and that is genuinely worth something. But much anxiety, depression, and OCD grows out of shame and relational history, and those soften through human contact over time, not through information alone.

How do I know if I need psychotherapy rather than an app or self-help?

A good sign is when relief keeps wearing off — when the books, podcasts, and late-night chats help for a night and the same pattern returns. That usually means the work is relational, and it is time to be met by a person, not only managed.
