On OCD

Beyond ERP: How I Treat OCD for the Long Term

By Nicole Chew-Helbig · June 2026 · Singapore

Search the word OCD and you land, almost at once, on three letters: ERP. Exposure and response prevention. It is a good foundation. It is not where the work ends.

Short answer ERP — facing what you fear without performing the ritual — is the behavioural foundation of OCD treatment, and I respect it. But OCD is long-term work, and the behaviour is only the surface. I treat OCD by helping you understand what your symptoms are and where they came from. That understanding becomes assurance: the steadiness to face a compulsion, and to stop being frightened of your own mind. The aim is not a life scrubbed of every symptom. It is a life large enough to hold them — one you can live in fully, and even thrive.

If you have googled your way here, you know the script. Type in OCD treatment and within seconds you are reading about exposure and response prevention: sit with the discomfort, resist the ritual, let the anxiety crest and fall. ERP is well-researched and useful. For many people it is the right place to begin.

But a foundation is not a home. ERP teaches the body that the feared thing can be survived without the ritual. On its own, it rarely tells you why the ritual was there, what it was guarding, or what to do when the old fear comes knocking again, months later, in a hard season.

That is the part I care about. Not instead of ERP. Beyond it.

OCD is long-term work, and it deserves to be treated that way

For most people, OCD is a long-term condition. The research is honest about this: many improve with exposure work, and many relapse, because the fear that drives OCD does not vanish. It recedes, and it can return. A protocol aimed only at the symptom can hold while life is steady and slip when life is not.

This is not a mark against ERP. It is the nature of the thing. OCD took root over years, and it asks to be met over time, not defeated in a six-week course. Treat it as long-term work and relapse stops being a verdict on how hard you tried. It becomes something you were already prepared for.

I start by helping you understand your symptoms

Before I ask you to face anything, I want you to understand what you are facing.

Together we get curious about the shape of your OCD. What does the intrusive thought actually say? What is the feared outcome underneath the checking, the washing, the counting, the silent reviewing? When did the world first start to feel unsafe enough that your mind had to build a defence this elaborate?

Often, beneath the ritual, we find something that makes complete sense. A child who grew up where danger felt constant. A moment where something frightening happened and no one explained it. A nervous system that learned, early, that certainty was the only safe ground. Seen this way, OCD is no longer a senseless malfunction. It is a creative adjustment — the best solution a younger version of you could find for something that felt unbearable.

This is the teaching heart of the work. You come to know your symptoms from the inside: their nature, their logic, their likely origins. And knowing changes how you relate to them.

Understanding becomes assurance

Here is what understanding does that resistance alone cannot.

When you can see the frightened logic of your OCD, and not only feel its grip, you stop being terrified of your own thoughts. An intrusive thought is no longer a threat to be neutralised at any cost. It becomes something you recognise. Ah. There it is again. I know what this is, and I know what it is protecting.

That recognition is assurance. Assurance is what lets you face a compulsion without white-knuckling through it. You are not bracing against a mystery. You are choosing, from understanding, to let the urge rise and pass — because you know it for what it is, and you no longer believe it holds the power it claims.

This is why I do not treat exposure as a test of willpower. Willpower runs out. Understanding does not. The client who knows the root of their symptoms carries something ERP alone cannot hand them: the quiet confidence to live differently.

Living — and even thriving — alongside OCD

A lot of OCD treatment carries a quiet assumption that the goal is zero symptoms. No intrusive thoughts, no urges, a mind scrubbed clean. I do not work towards that, and I do not think it is the most freeing aim.

The people I see do their best work when the symptoms lose their authority, not when they vanish. An intrusive thought can arrive and not ruin the afternoon. An urge can surface and pass without obedience. You notice the old fear, nod to it, and carry on with the meeting, the dinner, the life.

Many people do more than cope this way. They thrive. The same mind that produces the intrusive thoughts is often sensitive, vivid, deeply caring. Once it is no longer ruled by fear, that sensitivity becomes a gift. The aim is not a war you spend your life fighting. It is a life large enough that the symptoms become a small, manageable part of it.

What the work looks like over time

So the work is patient, and it is relational. We build understanding first, so that any exposure you do is chosen rather than endured. We tend to the fear underneath the ritual, in the safety of a relationship, so it can be felt and metabolised rather than managed forever. And we prepare, openly, for the seasons when symptoms flare, so that when they do, you meet them as something familiar.

ERP builds the foundation. Understanding builds the assurance. And assurance is what lets the change hold.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to understand it — and to live differently.

Frequently asked

Do you use ERP to treat OCD?

ERP — exposure and response prevention — is the behavioural foundation most OCD treatment is built on, and I respect what it can do. But ERP is not the whole of my work. I treat OCD by first helping you understand what your symptoms are and where they may come from, so that facing a compulsion is something you choose from understanding, not something you white-knuckle through. ERP is a tool. Understanding is what makes it last.

Can OCD be treated without ERP?

OCD can be treated with more than ERP alone. ERP works on the behaviour — the checking, washing, counting, avoiding. My work goes underneath the behaviour to the fear it is managing, helps you understand why the pattern formed, and builds the inner assurance that lets you live differently. For most people, lasting change needs both the behavioural foundation and the deeper understanding.

Why does OCD come back after ERP?

OCD is largely a long-term condition, and ERP that targets only the symptom can fade when life gets stressful and the old fear returns. If you never came to understand what the ritual was protecting you from, there is nothing to hold the change in place. When you understand the root of your symptoms, relapse stops feeling like failure and starts being something you can meet with assurance.

How long does OCD treatment take?

OCD is best understood as long-term work, not a short course you finish. Some people feel relief within weeks as the fear is named and understood; the deeper work of relating differently to your mind unfolds over months. The goal is not a quick fix but a durable change in your relationship to your own symptoms.

Can you live a normal life with OCD?

Yes. Many people live well with OCD, and some thrive. My aim is not to wage war on every symptom, but to loosen the grip of fear so that an intrusive thought or an urge no longer dictates your day. You can build a full, meaningful life while the occasional symptom comes and goes, and find it has far less power over you.

Does understanding the cause of OCD actually help?

It helps a great deal. When you understand the nature of your symptoms and their possible origins, the OCD stops feeling like a senseless invader and starts making sense as something your mind once built to cope. That understanding is what gives you the assurance to face compulsions, and to stop being frightened of your own thoughts.
