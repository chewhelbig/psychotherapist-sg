Why Don't Therapists Just Tell You the Answer?

By Nicole Chew-Helbig · April 2026

A version of this question keeps surfacing in online therapy communities. The frustration is real. So is the pattern. Here's what I'd say to it.

Some therapists won't tell you what they think. Some will, but only because you pushed and they caved. Neither of those is therapy.

The honest version is more boring than either. It depends on whether the therapist is actually wondering with you, or running a script.

The doctrine most of us were trained in says: the client has to find their own answer. Self-direction. Autonomy. Don't impose. There's a real reason behind it — therapists who hand down verdicts can flatten people, miss the texture of what's actually going on, mistake their own projections for clinical insight. Restraint is part of the craft.

But restraint can become reflex. "What do you think?" starts arriving in places where it doesn't belong — when someone is asking, very directly, whether what their partner did was abuse, or whether the way they were raised was normal, or whether they should leave the job that's making them sick. If the therapist lobs the question back at moments like that without any real wondering of their own, they're not being neutral. They're hiding.

The mirror failure is the therapist who gives answers because they feel pressured to. The client wants something definite, the therapist senses the pull, and produces a reading they don't actually hold — or hold only loosely — to relieve the pressure in the room. That isn't authenticity either. The client gets a verdict that came from the therapist's discomfort, not from contact with what was being asked.

What I try to do, when a client asks me a real question, is give them my version of the answer — and mention that it might land the wrong way. Here is what I see. It may not fit you. Tell me what doesn't. That isn't withholding and it isn't pronouncing. It's offering something I actually think, with the door left open.

The contract that makes this work, when it works: the client agrees the answer may not fit them. They're not asking for a verdict. They're asking for what's in the room with them. And what's in the room is another mind, with reactions, with a sense of things — not a diagnostic instrument.

So if your therapist never tells you what they think, and you're tired of throwing balls back across the room, that frustration is real. And if your therapist always tells you what to think, and you've started to feel managed rather than met — that's also information.

What you can ask for, plainly: I want to know what you actually think. Not the version where you ask me what I think. The other version. And I won't take it as the final word.

Some therapists will rise to it. Others won't. Both answers tell you something.
