Who Comes to Therapy

By Nicole Chew-Helbig, PhD · Gestalt Psychotherapist, Singapore

People ask me who comes to therapy. The honest answer is: people. A couple who have run out of ways to say the same thing. A mother who cannot remember the last time she finished a sentence about herself. A student whose grades are excellent and whose sleep is not. A man who has handled everything alone for six years and has started to wonder what the handling is costing him.

There is no type. There is no threshold of suffering you must cross before the door opens. Some arrive in crisis. Many arrive with something quieter — a sense that the life they are living has drifted slightly off the life they meant to live, and they cannot name where the drift began.

What they come for is also simpler than the brochures suggest. They come to be met. Not informed, not reassured, not given a worksheet — met. Received by another person who stays with what is actually happening rather than rushing past it. In a world that processes us constantly, that turns out to be rare.

In my room in Tiong Bahru, the work looks like a conversation. Unhurried. Sometimes Mulan is there, asleep near the chairs, entirely unimpressed by credentials. We start with where you are. Not where you think you should be, not where the self-help books say you ought to be by now. Where you are.

Over the coming months I am writing for the people who sit in these chairs — couples, parents, students, singles looking for love, people wrestling with their faith, people carrying their ageing parents and their children at the same time. Each piece is about what therapy is for, in that particular life.

If one of them sounds like yours, that is a reasonable place to begin.
