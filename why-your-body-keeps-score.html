Trauma & The Body

Why Your Body Keeps Score — Even When Your Mind Moves On

The body remembers what the mind has decided to move past.

You've processed it. You've talked about it. You understand why it happened.

So why does your chest still tighten when you hear that tone of voice? Why do your shoulders carry tension that no amount of massage resolves? Why does your stomach knot before every meeting with your boss, even though you know — rationally — that you're safe?

Because the body remembers what the mind has decided to move past.

The Body as Memory

We tend to think of memory as a mental function — images, narratives, facts stored in the brain. But the body has its own memory system. Muscles that brace in anticipation of a blow that happened years ago. A nervous system that stays on high alert long after the threat has passed. Breath that becomes shallow in situations that echo old overwhelm.

This isn't metaphor. It's physiology.

When we experience something overwhelming — especially if it happens repeatedly, or during childhood — the body's stress response gets stuck in a loop. Even when circumstances change, the body continues responding as though the old danger is still present. The body that decided I am in danger before the child had words cannot be argued out of its verdict by the adult who has acquired them.

How It Shows Up

Body-held stress and trauma can manifest in ways that seem unrelated to their cause: chronic pain, insomnia, digestive problems, skin conditions, weakened immunity, jaw clenching, persistent fatigue despite adequate rest.

Many people spend years treating these as purely medical issues — which they are, in part. But when the body is carrying unprocessed emotional experience, addressing only the physical symptoms leaves the deeper source untouched.

What I Pay Attention To

Gestalt therapy has always understood that we are not minds riding around in bodies. We are embodied beings.

In session, I pay attention not just to what you're saying but to how your body responds as you say it. The tightening of a fist. A sudden shift in posture. A breath held mid-sentence. The hand that goes to the throat — the body's instinct to protect the most vulnerable surface.

These aren't random. They're communications — the body telling us something that words haven't yet caught up to. By bringing gentle awareness to these signals, we create space for what's held in the body to be acknowledged, felt, and gradually released.

Trauma Processing

For some experiences, talk therapy alone isn't enough. This is where EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) can be transformative. EMDR works with the body's own processing mechanisms to help "unstick" traumatic memories that the nervous system hasn't been able to integrate.

The result isn't that you forget what happened. It's that the memory loses its charge — it becomes something that happened to you, rather than something that's still happening in your body.

Even when we ignore our distress, our bodies don't.
