Gestalt Therapy · The Whole of Many Parts

Gestalt Therapy:
The Whole of Many Parts

IFS names parts. EFT names emotion. DBT names dialectic. Somatic work names the body. These are parts of Gestalt therapy, the origin that holds the whole.

There are many therapies on offer in Singapore. IFS, EFT, DBT, EMDR, somatic experiencing, CBT, schema therapy. Each names something real about being human. That we have parts. That emotion organises us. That the body holds what the mind has not yet caught up with. That the past is unfinished until something contacts it.

Each of these is derived from Gestalt therapy. Gestalt is where these originate, and is broader. It is not one technique among others. It is a way of holding the whole — body, emotion, parts, past, room, relationship — at once.

What do I mean by “the whole”? Gestalt therapy’s foundational claim is that the whole is different from the sum of its parts. A clock is a whole. Its casing, hands, mechanism, battery, and face are parts. Each part has a function. The clock is something more than the parts assembled. Gestalt is the clock.

I wrote this piece for two readers. For people considering therapy who are trying to make sense of what’s on offer, and for fellow practitioners who are sometimes asked to defend their orientation in a landscape that rewards branding over depth. What follows is the philosophical ground I stand on — and how each of the major modalities lives inside it.

Three Commitments

Phenomenology is the discipline of attending to what presents itself before we explain it away. When a client tells me he is “in a bad place,” I notice it. I write it down. I let the session go on. I resist the pull to classify the statement. I notice instead the quality of his voice, the metaphor he has chosen, the atmosphere that gathers around those words. It may be months before either of us understands what “that place” actually is. My job is to stay close to the figure without forcing it to declare itself prematurely. This is what Staemmler (2009) calls the willingness to be uncertain. It is uncomfortable. It is also where the real work lives.

Field theory means the person is never understood in isolation. Organism and environment are a single reality. This sounds abstract until you sit in a room with a couple and feel the invisible architecture of their relationship pressing on your chest. Francesetti (2015) has taken this further: suffering does not reside inside the patient. It arises between persons, in the lived situation of their encounter. When I feel dread reading a client’s intake documents, that dread is data — field data, co-created in the space between what I know and what I am about to encounter.

Creative indifference — Friedlaender’s (1918) Schöpferische Indifferenz — is widely misunderstood. It is not neutrality. It is a dynamic stance at the centre between polarities, holding both without collapsing into either. When I sit with a couple on the verge of divorce, one begging to stay, the other already gone — I do not take sides. I hold the whole of what is presenting itself and trust that something will emerge from the centre. Beisser’s (1970) paradoxical theory of change is the clinical expression: transformation occurs through the full acceptance of what is, not through forcing the organism to be different.

In Dialogue With Seven Approaches

Seven Approaches Inside Gestalt

Each of the major contemporary modalities names something Gestalt has been working with for decades. What follows is how each lives inside the Gestalt frame — what it brought into clearer focus, and how Gestalt holds it within a larger field.

Internal Family Systems (IFS)

IFS and Gestalt both recognise that human experience is polyphonic. IFS gave parts work a clear vocabulary — protectors, managers, firefighters, exiles. Gestalt has worked with these same phenomena for decades as fixed Gestalts: rigid patterns of creative adjustment that once served survival and have become frozen. The topdog/underdog chair, the integration with psychodrama, the dialogue between selves — these are part work in Gestalt’s house style.

The framing differs. Gestalt asks “What field conditions call forth this pattern?” as well as “What does this part need?” Self in Gestalt is process rather than essence — a system of contacts, always in formation. Change depends on fresh contact with what is here, not on accessing a buried essence. Gestalt does not assign names to parts. They are not discrete identities. They are processes of the self in relation.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

CBT begins with cognition. Its premise is that distress arises from distorted thinking that can be identified and corrected. The philosophical distance from Gestalt is real. When a client says “I am worthless,” a CBT therapist might identify a core belief to challenge. In Gestalt I stay with the phenomenon. How does worthlessness live in this person’s body right now? What atmosphere does it create between us? I do not presume to know better than the client what their experience means. We discover what the phenomenon means together over time, because meaning also changes.

The convergence has grown. Mindfulness-based CBT has moved closer to the phenomenological attitude — inviting clients to observe their thoughts without pre-judgment. This is the place where CBT has been finding its way toward what Gestalt has always called awareness. And in Gestalt, homework lives as experiment — a creative process rather than a prescription.

Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT)

DBT’s central dialectic — the tension between acceptance and change — mirrors Gestalt’s paradoxical theory of change: transformation comes through the full acceptance of what is, not through forcing the organism to be different. The resonance is genuine and old. Gestalt has held this dialectic since Friedlaender’s creative indifference (1918) and Beisser’s paradoxical theory (1970).

The mechanism differs. DBT teaches structured coping skills. Gestalt trusts that when the organism is fully aware of its present situation, self-regulation emerges from within. Change is discovered rather than learned. For the populations DBT claims to serve best — borderline features, severe dysregulation — the relational holding Gestalt offers can be the load-bearing piece. It relies on the presence and attention of the therapist, attuned to the suffering behind the condition: shame and rupture in contact.

EMDR

EMDR proposes that traumatic memories are stored in unprocessed form and that bilateral stimulation facilitates their integration. The notion that trauma remains unfinished is itself a Gestalt concept — the open Gestalt that Perls and Goodman wrote about decades before EMDR was named. Both approaches agree that trauma must be contacted, not merely discussed.

Where they sit differently: EMDR is manualised. Gestalt exists in the relational field — in the lived encounter between a client and a therapist who is genuinely present and genuinely willing to be disturbed. Manualisation is a form of hiding behind the protocol; Gestalt is about presence.

The bilateral stimulation EMDR uses helps keep the client grounded during intense recall of traumatic experience. It can be done with tapping or eye movement. What the therapy is really about, though, is the presence of a practitioner who can hear it.

Somatic Therapy

Somatic approaches derive from Gestalt a commitment to the body, rejecting the Cartesian split. The alignment is deep — Gestalt has been embodied since Perls’ line about losing your mind and coming to your senses.

The distinction is subtle but consequential. Many somatic approaches treat the body as a site of stored trauma to be discharged or regulated through specific techniques. Gestalt’s relationship to the body is more radical: the body is the ground from which figure emerges. It is trusted as the organism’s means of creative contact with its environment, not as a mechanism to be regulated. The most transformative moments in my work arise from atmospheric shifts that occur when genuine contact is made in the somatic and relational space of the therapeutic session.

Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)

EFT, grounded in attachment theory, has the most natural affinity with Gestalt of any of these approaches — Greenberg trained as a Gestalt therapist before developing EFT. In my couples work (Chew-Helbig, 2026), I have explored how attachment patterns manifest as field phenomena: a tightening grip, a gradual withdrawal, the atmospheric quality of abandonment filling the room.

EFT pursues secure attachment as a therapeutic goal. Gestalt pursues no predetermined state — it attends to the phenomenon of the moment and attunes to the pathos of the field. Jaspers (1913/1997) understood this: psychopathology is the study of the soul’s suffering, not the diagnosis of mental illness. EFT and Gestalt sit closely together, often in the same couples session.

Psychoanalysis

The oldest conversation here. Perls trained as an analyst, and Gestalt grew partly as creative rebellion against psychoanalysis’s excessive intellectualism. The family resemblance runs deep — Freud’s structural model, Klein’s object relations, Winnicott’s transitional space all sit somewhere inside Gestalt’s lineage.

The temporal axis differs. Psychoanalysis works through the past; Gestalt works in the present, treating whatever history is alive in the room as the only history available. Contemporary relational psychoanalysis has moved closer to that ground — the Boston Change Process Study Group’s (2010) “moments of meeting” describe something remarkably similar to what Gestalt has always called contact. The two traditions are increasingly speaking the same language.

Historical Context

A Timeline — And What It Reveals

Psychoanalysis stands at the beginning. Freud published The Interpretation of Dreams in 1900. Everything else follows from — or reacts to — that original conversation about the unconscious, the body, and human suffering.

Year Modality Founder(s) Trained In
1900 Psychoanalysis Sigmund Freud (1900) Neurology
1951 Gestalt Therapy Fritz Perls, Laura Perls, Paul Goodman (1951) Psychoanalysis
1960s CBT Aaron Beck (1967) Psychoanalysis
~1985 IFS Richard Schwartz (1995) Systemic family therapy
~1985 EFT Sue Johnson (1985) Humanistic & systemic therapy
1987 EMDR Francine Shapiro (1989) English literature & behavioural therapy
Late 1980s Somatic Experiencing Peter Levine (1997) Biophysics & psychology
1993 DBT Marsha Linehan (1993) Behavioural psychology

Gestalt therapy was not a rejection of psychoanalysis. It was its completion — an attempt to restore the body, the present moment, and the relational field that classical analysis had intellectualised away.

CBT moved in a fundamentally different direction. Beck left the consulting room’s depth for the laboratory’s measurability. CBT is not psychotherapy in the relational sense. It is a behavioural intervention with a cognitive frame. Effective for what it targets. But it does not sit in the same philosophical lineage.

Then came the branded modalities — five of them within a single decade, all by clinicians trained in earlier traditions, all repackaging elements that already existed in humanistic, existential, and body-based approaches. Writing this, we might question the relevance of focussing on these techniques alone in today's post pandemic, multicultural world, where Artificial Intelligence is part of our psychological ecosystem.

This is not a dismissal. Each of these modalities has contributed clinical innovations in the past, and reflect primarily Western cultures. DBT’s work with severe dysregulation is genuinely important. EMDR has pin-pointed the use of physical stimulation as part of the process while facilitating access of traumatic material. EFT brought attachment theory into the couples room, a re-iteration of what psychoanalysis had already brought to light through the concept of transference.

But it is worth noticing the pattern. A clinician encounters a limitation in adopting the modality they were trained in. They discover something — often something that phenomenological and humanistic therapies have practised for decades — and build a branded protocol around it. Many also recognise a marketing opportunity. In the United States more than elsewhere, rebranding is a thing, a way of colonialisation. The protocol acquires a training institute, a certification process, a trademark. The insight becomes a product. Branding is commercial, and it tends to carry an implicit message: that this particular way of working is superior to others.

The net result is no enhancement to the mental profession, but a dumbing down of what psychotherapists already have, a rich philosophical inheritance from psychoanalysis.

That message is not supported by the evidence. Over thirty years of psychotherapy research — most notably the work of Bruce Wampold and the common factors literature — has consistently shown that therapeutic outcomes are not modality-specific. They are therapist-specific. What determines whether therapy works is the quality of the therapeutic relationship, the skill and presence of the therapist, and the client’s active participation — not the brand name on the door (Wampold, 2001, 2015; Wampold & Imel, 2015; Norcross & Lambert, 2018). Some modalities invest heavily in funding research to demonstrate their superiority. But when bona fide therapies are compared head to head, the differences between them are negligible. The differences between therapists are not.

The Landscape

Why So Many Modalities?

Each branded modality has named a useful aspect that originates from Gestalt therapy. These parts are useful.

IFS gave a generation of clinicians a clear vocabulary for parts of the self. Gestalt has worked with parts for decades — the topdog/underdog chair, the integration with psychodrama, the dialogue between selves.

EMDR gave a structured way to do something that less structured therapies had been doing for decades — staying with what the system has not finished. In Gestalt this work happens without a manual. The therapist listens for the client’s process and stays with it as it unfolds. The contact is responsive to the person, not to a protocol.

DBT gave practitioners working with severe distress a set of tools that genuinely help people who weren’t being well served. Gestalt arrives at this work through experiments — interventions tailored to this client in this moment, that the client can try, modify, or reject. The therapist’s creativity is part of the method; so is the client’s authorship over what works.

Each contribution has its function. Each is also a part of a larger whole that already has a name.

What is also true is that the names are newer than the work. Most of what these modalities do can be traced back to humanistic, existential, body-based, and analytic approaches that pre-date them by decades. Each branded modality acquires a training institute, a certification process, a trademark. The insight becomes a product. Branding is commercial, and it tends to carry an implicit message: that this particular way of working is uniquely effective.

There is also something the branded modalities cannot teach: culture. The person across from you carries a cultural world that no certification programme can fully prepare you for. This is knowledge that matters more than technique. It is learnable only through sustained, attentive clinical work with the people who walk through your door.

Psychotherapy is psychotherapy. The branded modalities are useful as parts. The whole already has a name, and a tradition that has been thinking about contact, body, field, and presence for over a century.

At a Glance

Five Questions Across Eight Modalities

Five questions, seven modalities. The Gestalt column holds steady throughout.

What is the self?

Gestalt: A process — not a fixed thing but a living system of contacts that forms between people. It is situational, flexible, and always in motion.

IFS: A core essence — the “Self” is inherently compassionate, curious, calm; separate from parts.

CBT: A cognitive agent with beliefs, schemas, and automatic thoughts.

DBT: An individual with skills deficits requiring psychoeducation.

EMDR: A neurological system with adaptive and maladaptive memory networks.

Somatic: An organism with a nervous system that stores and processes traumatic experience.

EFT: Shaped by early attachment; organised around internal working models.

Psychoanalysis: Structured by unconscious forces (classical); relational configurations (contemporary).

Where does suffering live?

Gestalt: In the field — between persons, in the atmosphere of the shared situation.

IFS: In exiled parts — wounded inner personalities carrying burdens from the past.

CBT: In distorted cognitions that misrepresent reality.

DBT: In insufficient capacity to regulate emotions and tolerate distress.

EMDR: In unprocessed traumatic memories stored dysfunctionally in neural networks.

Somatic: In the body — as stored activation, frozen survival responses, autonomic dysregulation.

EFT: In insecure attachment patterns established in childhood.

Psychoanalysis: In unconscious conflict, repressed material, and early relational templates.

How does change happen?

Gestalt: Through fresh contact with what is actually here — in the body, in the room, between people. When awareness is restored, the organism self-regulates. The person does not need to be told what to do. They begin to move toward what they need.

IFS: Through Self-leadership negotiating with, witnessing, and unburdening parts.

CBT: Through identification and replacement of cognitive distortions.

DBT: Through balancing acceptance and change; acquiring structured coping skills.

EMDR: Through bilateral stimulation activating adaptive information processing.

Somatic: Through titration, pendulation, and discharge of stored activation.

EFT: Through accessing primary emotions; restructuring attachment bonds.

Psychoanalysis: Through insight (classical); through new relational knowing (contemporary).

What is the therapist’s position?

Gestalt: Phenomenological witness — present in the client’s life-space, not outside it. The therapist is shaped by the field and shapes it. Observation and participation are not separate acts.

IFS: Facilitator of inner dialogue between the client’s Self and their parts.

CBT: Expert diagnostician of faulty thinking; guides restructuring.

DBT: Teacher and coach; validates while pushing for behavioural change.

EMDR: Technician guiding a neurological process; monitors and facilitates.

Somatic: Skilled tracker of the client’s nervous system; guides somatic interventions.

EFT: Attachment-informed guide; creates safety to access vulnerable emotions.

Psychoanalysis: Interpreter of unconscious material (classical); acknowledges countertransference (contemporary).

What is the therapeutic goal?

Gestalt: Awareness — brought about through contact and dialogue. Like lighting up a life-space that was previously in darkness. When the client can see clearly, they know where to go. The goal may change. The capacity to see is permanent.

IFS: Self-leadership — the core Self governing the internal system with compassion.

CBT: Rational thinking; symptom reduction through cognitive correction.

DBT: A “life worth living” through emotional regulation and interpersonal effectiveness.

EMDR: Desensitisation and adaptive reprocessing of traumatic material.

Somatic: Regulation of the autonomic nervous system; completing thwarted survival responses.

EFT: Secure attachment; emotional bonding through expression of unmet needs.

Psychoanalysis: Making the unconscious conscious (classical); expanded capacity for relating (contemporary).

The Whole

The Whole of Many Parts

After years of practice and research, I have come to understand that Gestalt therapy is best described by what it holds, not by what it does. It holds the body. It holds the relationship between us. It holds the field — the cultural and lived world the client is already inside. It holds the parts — protectors, exiles, the rigid patterns that were once creative adjustments to survival. It holds the past, not as archaeology, but in how it shapes what is happening right now in this room.

Each modality named in this article contributes parts of the whole. CBT’s rigour. IFS’s vocabulary for parts. EFT’s relational depth. DBT’s pragmatism with severe distress. EMDR’s structured contact with unfinished trauma. Somatic therapy’s fidelity to the body. Psychoanalysis’s willingness to sit with complexity. Gestalt therapy holds these as parts of a single conversation, without elevating any one as the whole.

Gestalt therapy has no trademark. No single founder who controlled its dissemination. No certification empire. It is a philosophical orientation that has been practised, argued about, and refined by a community for over seventy years. That is a different kind of thing. It cannot be marketed in the way a branded modality can — and it also cannot be diluted in the way a branded modality can.

My choice of practice is not a brand. It is a way of being with another person — informed by philosophy, shaped by thousands of hours of clinical work, refined by the cultural realities of the people I sit with. The map is not the territory. The living moment between two people, with all its pathos and creative potential, remains the only place real change has ever happened.

The whole has many parts. They are already here in the room when we sit down.
