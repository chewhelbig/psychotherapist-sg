On Relationships

The Difference Between Being Heard and Being Agreed With

Most arguments aren't about who's right. They're about who feels heard.

In couples therapy, I watch this happen constantly. One partner shares something vulnerable. The other responds by explaining why they disagree, or defending their position, or offering a solution.

The first partner shuts down. The conversation collapses. Both feel frustrated.

What went wrong? The second partner heard the words — but they didn't hear the person.

What "Being Heard" Actually Means

Being heard doesn't mean the other person agrees with you. It doesn't mean they'll change their behaviour. It doesn't mean you're right and they're wrong.

It means: I see that this matters to you. I understand that you're feeling something real. I'm not dismissing it.

That's it. And it changes everything.

Why We Confuse the Two

Because most of us grew up in environments where agreement was the only form of validation. If someone disagreed with you, it meant they didn't care. If they didn't see it your way, you felt invisible.

So we fight for agreement when what we actually need is acknowledgment.

In the Therapy Room

Some of the most transformative moments in couples therapy happen when one partner stops trying to be right and simply says: "I hear you. I see that this hurts."

Not "I agree." Not "You're right and I'm wrong." Just: I'm here. I see you.

This is what contact means in Gestalt therapy. Not merging, not capitulating — meeting. Two separate people, each with their own experience, willing to be in the same room without one having to disappear.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
