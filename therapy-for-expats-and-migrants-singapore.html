On Belonging

Therapy for Expats and Migrants in Singapore

Living between cultures can be enriching. It can also be profoundly lonely.

Singapore is full of people who came from somewhere else. Expats, migrants, trailing spouses, third-culture kids. The surface is cosmopolitan. Underneath, many are quietly struggling with displacement, loneliness, and the question of where they belong.

What Displacement Feels Like

It's not always dramatic. It's the slow erosion of identity that comes from living in a place where no one knows your history. It's the performance of being "fine" at expat gatherings while feeling profoundly disconnected. It's the guilt of missing home and the guilt of not missing it enough.

For some, the move triggers old wounds — separation anxiety, abandonment, the feeling of never quite fitting in. The geography is new, but the emotional landscape is painfully familiar.

My Background

I grew up in a multicultural Chinese-Eurasian family in Singapore, lived and trained in Vienna, worked with asylum seekers during the refugee crisis in Austria, and returned to practise here. I know what it means to live between cultures — to feel at home and not at home simultaneously.

My practice is open to anyone regardless of nationality, religion, sexual orientation, or gender identity. All sessions are fully confidential.

What Therapy Can Offer

A place to speak honestly about the experience of displacement — without minimising it, without being told you should be grateful, without having to perform resilience.

Therapy can help you grieve what you left behind, make sense of who you're becoming in this new context, and find ground beneath your feet — even when the geography keeps shifting.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
