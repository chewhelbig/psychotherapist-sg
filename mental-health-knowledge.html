Mental Health Knowledge

Diagnosis & Medication

Two of the questions people bring most often to therapy. Honest answers from a psychotherapist's chair — about what a diagnosis is and isn't, whether you need medication, and how therapy works alongside a doctor's care.

People arrive with questions they've been turning over for a while. Should I get a diagnosis? Does the label mean something is permanently wrong with me? Do I need medication, or am I avoiding the real work if I take it? Will the therapist judge me either way?

These are good questions, and they deserve straight answers. What follows is how I think about diagnosis and medication as a Gestalt psychotherapist in Singapore. A note before we start: I'm a psychotherapist and SAC-registered counsellor, not a medical doctor. I don't give formal diagnoses or prescribe medication — those belong with a psychiatrist, a GP, or a clinical psychologist. This page is for understanding, not medical advice. For a decision about your own diagnosis or medication, speak with a doctor who knows your situation.

Part One

On Diagnosis

Do I need a diagnosis to start therapy?

No. You don't need a diagnosis, a referral, or a name for what you're going through to begin. Many people come simply because something is off — they're anxious, exhausted, stuck, grieving — and they want to understand it. We can start from exactly there.

Can you give me a diagnosis?

A formal psychiatric diagnosis comes from a psychiatrist — a medical doctor — or, for many conditions, from a clinical psychologist through assessment. As a psychotherapist and counsellor, I don't issue formal diagnoses or prescribe medication. What I do is work with your experience as it actually is, and look for the root of the symptoms. Where medication alleviates symptoms, therapy goes after what's underneath them — discovering, or rediscovering, the root, so that you can see the patterns for yourself and make changes on your own agency. If I think a diagnostic assessment or a medication review would help you, I'll say so, and I can point you toward the right professional.

What's the difference between a diagnosis and understanding myself?

A diagnosis is a shared label that groups symptoms together so professionals can communicate and plan treatment. It's genuinely useful — but it describes a category, not a person, and the same diagnosis is a different story in different people. Therapy works holistically. Good therapy often doesn't need the diagnostic label at all, because we focus on your actual experiences and relationships — and that's what helps unpack the stuckness that keeps symptoms going and, left alone, makes them worse.

Is a diagnosis permanent? Does it define me?

A diagnosis is a description of a pattern at a point in time — not a life sentence and not an identity. Diagnoses get revised as circumstances change and as more is understood about you. It's a working tool. It can open doors to the right help, but it is never the final word on who you are.

I think I might have ADHD, autism, or bipolar — should I get assessed?

It depends on the person. Some people feel better getting a diagnosis — it brings clarity, relief, and sometimes access to treatment or accommodations. Others don't need one to do the work. And some are harmed by it, because the label hardens into an identity — something they then live as, rather than something that simply describes a pattern. If the question keeps returning, it's worth taking seriously rather than carrying alone; a proper assessment with a psychiatrist or clinical psychologist is the place to settle it, and therapy can run alongside, making sense of what it means for your life.

Why did two professionals give me different impressions?

It happens. Human experience doesn't always sort neatly into categories, presentations overlap, and people show different sides in different rooms. A difference in view isn't a sign that someone got it wrong — it's a reminder that a diagnosis is a map, not the territory. What matters is whether the understanding helps you get the right support.

Part Two

On Medication

My guiding principle is simple: as little as possible, and as much as needed.

Do I need medication to get better?

The honest answer starts with that principle — as little as possible, as much as needed. If you can still work and play, if daily life is hard but holding, therapy is often enough on its own. And there's something to that: people tend to feel better and more empowered when the change comes from the work they've done themselves, rather than from a tablet alone. Medication has a real place — particularly when symptoms are severe, unrelenting, or making daily life unworkable — but it also carries side effects, so it's worth using deliberately rather than by default. The prescribing decision belongs with a doctor or psychiatrist. This is simply the lens I'd bring to that conversation.

Can therapy and medication work together?

Yes — and often they work best together. Medication can lower the intensity of symptoms so there's room to feel and think; therapy works with the meaning, the patterns, and what's underneath them. Many of the people I see take medication prescribed by a psychiatrist or GP and do the relational work with me in parallel. The two aren't in competition.

Will you tell me to start or stop my medication?

No. Starting, adjusting, or stopping medication is a medical decision, made with the doctor who prescribed it. Stopping suddenly can be risky and is best done with medical guidance. What I can do is help you notice and put words to your experience on medication — what's easing, what's flattening, what's changed — so the conversation with your prescriber is better informed.

Who can prescribe in Singapore?

Psychiatrists — medical doctors with specialist training in mental health — diagnose and prescribe. Some GPs prescribe for common presentations of anxiety and depression and refer on when a case is more complex. Clinical psychologists assess and treat through therapy but don't prescribe, and neither do counsellors or psychotherapists. If you're not sure where to start, a GP you trust is often a sensible first door.

Does medication fix the underlying problem?

No. Medication works on symptoms — it can quiet the alarm, lift the floor, steady the swing. Sometimes that is exactly what's needed, and there's no virtue in suffering through something that medicine can ease. But it doesn't resolve the relationships, the history, and the meanings a symptom is bound up with. Therapy can — by looking for the roots, through insight, experimentation, exposure, dialogue, and pattern change. That work takes time, but it's the part that lasts.

Is there something therapy can reach that medication can't?

Yes — and shame is the clearest example. Much of what brings people to therapy sits underneath the symptoms: the quiet conviction that there is something wrong not with what you did, but with who you are — that if you were truly seen, you'd be found wanting. Shame isn't a chemical state sealed inside you. It's relational. It forms early, in the faces that turned away or didn't quite meet us, and it can only reorganise in the presence of another person who stays — who doesn't flinch, correct, or leave when you're most exposed.

This is the work medicine, by its nature, leaves out. Medication can lower the distress that surrounds shame, and that can help. But a tablet cannot be the witness. It cannot be moved by you, cannot rupture and repair, cannot risk being changed by the encounter. Processing shame — being exposed and met anyway — is what actually heals it, and it requires a living, fallible Other in the room. That is precisely what therapy offers and what no medication, and no machine, can provide.

Will medication change who I am?

This is a common and reasonable worry. The aim of treatment is the opposite of erasing you — it's to turn down the noise of symptoms so more of you can be present, not less. If medication leaves you feeling flat, numbed, or not yourself, that isn't something to endure quietly; it's important information to bring back to your prescriber, who can adjust the type or the dose.

Can I do therapy instead of medication?

For many people, yes — particularly when symptoms are mild to moderate and daily life is still workable. Therapy asks more of you than swallowing a tablet does, but that's part of why it tends to leave people feeling more capable: the steadiness you build is your own, not borrowed from a dose. For more severe or persistent conditions, the evidence favours doing both, at least for a season. The aim isn't to be on the least medication as a point of pride, or the most out of caution — it's the right amount for as long as it's genuinely needed, alongside work that lets you need less of it over time.
