On Shame

Shame and Why It Matters in Therapy

The emotion we spend our lives fleeing may be the one that makes us most human.

Shame is the emotion nobody wants to talk about. Not guilt — guilt says "I did something wrong." Shame says "I am something wrong."

It is one of the earliest emotions we experience, and one of the most powerful forces shaping how we live, relate, and hide.

Why Shame Shows Up in Therapy

Almost every client who walks into my room carries shame. They may not name it. They may present with anxiety, depression, relationship difficulties, addiction. But when we slow down and pay attention, shame is often sitting beneath everything.

The person who can't ask for what they need — shame taught them their needs were too much.

The person who overworks compulsively — shame told them they had to earn their place.

The person who can't let their partner see them cry — shame convinced them that vulnerability is weakness.

Shame Is Not the Enemy

In my work, I've come to see shame differently. It is not simply a wound to be healed or a distortion to be corrected. Shame is a signal — of our social nature, our need for belonging, our deep awareness that we exist in relation to others.

The problem isn't that we feel shame. It's that we have no space to bring it into the room.

How We Work with Shame in Gestalt Therapy

We don't analyse shame from a distance. We meet it in the moment — when a client's voice drops, when they look away, when they say "this is stupid" before telling me something important.

The therapeutic relationship becomes the place where shame can be witnessed without being confirmed. Where someone can feel exposed and still be met with warmth.

This is not a technique. It is a way of being with another person that allows what was hidden to come into contact — gently, at the client's pace.

Shame in Relationships

In couples work, shame is often the invisible third party. Partners defend, withdraw, attack, or go silent — and underneath, shame is driving the bus. "If you really saw me, you wouldn't stay."

Some of the most transformative moments in couples therapy happen when one partner allows their shame to be visible — and the other responds not with judgment, but with recognition.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
