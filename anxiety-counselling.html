Anxiety Counselling in Singapore

What anxiety really feels like — and how therapy helps you find steadiness again.

Anxiety Doesn't Feel the Same for Everyone

Some people describe anxiety as a sudden tightening in the chest — the world closing in until there's barely room to breathe. Others live with it as a constant hum, a background vibration they've learned to function through but never fully escape.

For some, anxiety arrives as thoughts that won't stop: circling the same fears, reviewing the same possibilities, exhausting the mind. For others, it shows up in the body — sleepless nights, eczema flares, stomach knots, an immune system that collapses under the slightest stress.

When someone tells me they're anxious, I don't assume I know what they mean. I ask them to describe it. Because the experience matters.

Panic in the body

For many, anxiety comes as a wave of panic: the heart racing, breath tight and fast, a sudden sense of losing control. The world narrows to one overwhelming sensation — "Something's not right" or "I need to get out of here." Even when you know rationally you're safe, your body reacts as if danger is imminent.

The mind in overdrive

For others, anxiety lives primarily in the mind — spiralling thoughts, relentless worry, rumination, compulsive checking. The mind becomes a tireless companion, always preparing for the worst.

Fear, anticipation, and hypervigilance

Some experience anxiety as a subtle dread: a sense that something terrible is about to happen, that others are judging them, that they can't relax. The world feels unpredictable, unsafe. This kind of anxiety often goes unnamed for years. People call it "just being a worrier" or "being sensitive." It's more than that.

Anxiety & the Body

When the Body Carries What the Mind Can't Hold

The body doesn't lie. It can't. When anxiety runs long enough, it moves from something you feel into something your body becomes.

Insomnia

You're exhausted, but your nervous system won't let you sleep. The mind races the moment the lights go off. Or you fall asleep fine but wake at 3am, alert and agitated, unable to return. Sleep problems are one of the most common ways anxiety shows itself — and one of the most depleting.

Tension, headaches, jaw pain

Chronic tension in the shoulders, neck, and jaw. Headaches that come and go without clear cause. Teeth grinding at night. These are the body bracing — holding itself tight against a danger it hasn't been able to name or discharge.

Digestive issues

Stomach knots, nausea, IBS symptoms, appetite changes. The gut is dense with nerve endings and intimately connected to emotional states. Many people spend years seeing gastroenterologists before realising the source is emotional.

Skin flares and immune collapse

Eczema, psoriasis, hives, frequent illness. The immune system is regulated by the nervous system. When the nervous system is chronically activated, the body's defences weaken or misfire.

Chest tightness, shallow breathing, dizziness

The breath is the first thing anxiety changes. Shallow, fast breathing starves the body of oxygen and creates its own cascade of symptoms — lightheadedness, tingling, a sense of unreality. Many people end up in A&E convinced they're having a heart attack.

Even when we ignore our distress, our bodies don't. The body keeps score — and it speaks clearly if we learn to listen.

Anxiety & Other Conditions

Anxiety Rarely Travels Alone

Most people who come to my practice don't arrive saying "I have anxiety." They come with depression, burnout, relationship problems, compulsive behaviours. But when we slow down and look beneath the presenting issue, anxiety is often the engine running underneath.

Anxiety and depression

Depression is often what happens when anxiety has been running for too long. The nervous system, exhausted from months or years of hypervigilance, collapses. What looks like flatness, withdrawal, and loss of motivation is frequently the body shutting down after a prolonged state of alarm. Treating the depression without addressing the underlying anxiety means we're treating the crash without understanding the drive.

Anxiety and OCD

OCD is anxiety's attempt to create order in a world that feels unsafe. The hand-washing, the checking, the counting, the need for things to be symmetrical or "just right" — these are rituals the mind has developed to manage terror. The ritual provides momentary relief. Then the doubt returns, and the cycle begins again. In therapy, we don't just target the behaviour. We meet the fear underneath it.

Anxiety and burnout

Burnout is rarely just about workload. It's about what you believe will happen if you stop. Many people experiencing burnout have spent years running on anxiety-fuelled discipline — performing, delivering, proving their worth. The burnout arrives when the system can no longer sustain this pace. What collapses isn't just energy. It's meaning.

Anxiety and shame

Shame and anxiety are deeply intertwined. Shame says: "There is something fundamentally wrong with me." Anxiety responds: "If anyone sees it, I'm finished." Together they create a pattern of hiding, performing, and exhausting yourself trying to be acceptable. In my work — both in individual therapy and in my writing on shame — I've come to see this pairing as one of the most potent and least discussed drivers of psychological suffering.

Anxiety and relationship difficulties

Anxiety shapes how we love. The anxious partner who needs constant reassurance. The avoidant partner who withdraws when closeness feels threatening. The couple locked in a pursue-withdraw cycle that neither can name. In couples work, I often see two anxious people — each managing their anxiety through opposite strategies, each triggering the other's deepest fears.

Anxiety and compulsive behaviours

Compulsive eating, compulsive exercise, compulsive scrolling, compulsive working, compulsive sexual behaviour. These aren't character flaws. They are the nervous system's attempt to regulate itself — to find relief, however brief, from a state of chronic unease. Therapy doesn't simply remove the behaviour. It addresses the anxiety the behaviour was managing.

Where Anxiety Shows Up

Anxiety in Daily Life

Anxiety doesn't stay in a box. It follows you into every part of your life.

At work

Perfectionism that slows you down. Procrastination that isn't laziness but fear of failure. Difficulty speaking up in meetings. Over-preparing for everything. Saying yes to every request because you're afraid of being seen as inadequate. The Sunday night dread. The sense that no matter how much you do, it's never enough.

In relationships

Reading too much into a partner's silence. Avoiding conflict until it explodes. Needing constant reassurance but feeling ashamed of needing it. Picking partners who keep you at a distance because closeness feels dangerous. Or clinging so tightly that you push people away.

In school and study

Test anxiety. Blank mind during exams despite knowing the material. Difficulty concentrating. Comparing yourself endlessly to peers. The pressure to perform — from parents, from culture, from yourself — creating a cycle of preparation and dread.

As a parent

Constant worry about your children's safety, development, or future. Second-guessing every parenting decision. Losing your temper and then drowning in guilt. The weight of being responsible for another person when you can barely manage yourself.

In transitions

Moving to a new country. Starting a new job. A divorce. A bereavement. Retirement. Any major life transition can activate anxiety — especially if the transition echoes earlier experiences of loss, instability, or abandonment.

In stillness

Perhaps the most telling sign: the inability to be still. If rest feels dangerous, if silence makes you uneasy, if you need constant stimulation to feel okay — that is anxiety. It has become so familiar you no longer recognise it.

Anxiety & Trauma

When Anxiety Has Roots in the Past

Not all anxiety is about the present. Some anxiety is the nervous system still responding to something that happened long ago — an experience that was never fully processed, never made sense of, never completed.

What trauma does to the nervous system

Trauma changes the way the brain assesses danger. After a traumatic experience, the nervous system recalibrates — it becomes more sensitive, more reactive, faster to sound the alarm. What was once a survival response becomes a permanent state of readiness. You may not remember the original event clearly. But your body remembers. It responds to triggers — a tone of voice, a smell, a feeling of being trapped — with the same intensity it did originally.

Childhood and developmental trauma

Not all trauma is a single event. Growing up in an unpredictable household. A parent who was emotionally unavailable or volatile. Being bullied. Being the responsible child who held the family together. These experiences wire the nervous system for vigilance. The child who learned to scan the room for danger becomes the adult who can't relax at a dinner party.

How therapy works with trauma-related anxiety

We don't start with the trauma. We start with safety — building trust, establishing resources, helping your nervous system learn that it's safe to feel. Only when you're ready do we approach the traumatic material. Gestalt therapy works with trauma in the present tense — noticing what's happening in the body and the space between us as the past surfaces. EMDR can be powerfully effective for processing traumatic memories that remain "stuck."

How We Try to Calm Ourselves

Most of us find ways to manage anxiety long before we name it.

We scroll late into the night, letting the endless feed numb the thoughts. We work compulsively — staying late, filling every spare moment — because stillness feels dangerous. We exercise until exhaustion. We turn to alcohol, cannabis, or medication to finally feel quiet inside.

These aren't failures of character. They are attempts at self-regulation — strategies that work just well enough to delay the deeper work.

Gestalt Therapy for Anxiety

How I Work with Anxiety

There is no single protocol in my practice. Gestalt therapy is relational, experiential, and field-based. We work with you, not a diagnosis.

Most people move through recognisable phases — though not always in this order.

1. Awareness

Many people live with anxiety for so long that they stop noticing it until it overwhelms them. Therapy begins with awareness: sensing the small shifts in the body, the patterns in thinking, the early signs of escalation. Awareness itself can be regulating.

2. Emotional clarity

Anxiety is rarely alone. Shame, guilt, frustration, and self-judgment often sit beside it. We learn to distinguish these emotions, understand their messages, and soften the internal pressure to "just get over it."

3. Expression and experimentation

Gestalt therapy involves experimenting with new ways of being: speaking a boundary you've been afraid to set, allowing anger that has been suppressed, sitting with discomfort long enough to learn it won't shatter you. This isn't theoretical work. It's embodied, experiential, and relational.

4. Nervous system work

Understanding fight–flight–freeze responses, practising grounding techniques, and learning to identify activation states can offer powerful stability early in therapy.

5. Trauma processing

Anxiety often has roots in experiences that were never fully processed. As therapy deepens, we may work with trauma directly. EMDR can be transformative when appropriate.

6. Relational and ancestral patterns

Some anxiety isn't only personal — it's relational, familial, intergenerational. This is where constellation work can open insights that talk therapy alone cannot reach.

How Long Does This Take?

You might feel some relief after the first session: being heard is regulating in itself. But anxiety counselling is not a quick fix.

The aim isn't to eliminate anxiety. It's to help anxiety move from the foreground of your life to the background.

Most clients begin with weekly or biweekly sessions. As things stabilise, sessions become less frequent — monthly check-ins, or as needed when life intensifies.

Over time, you develop a responsive relationship with your anxiety rather than a reactive one.

What We're Working Toward

The Goal of Anxiety Therapy

The goal isn't to never feel anxious again. It's to feel more at ease in your own body. To sleep better. To support your immune system. To stay present with people you love. To respond rather than react. To feel more grounded in yourself.

Healthy relationships require this kind of presence — not the absence of anxiety, but the capacity to stay engaged even when it arises.

You're not broken. The work is helping you return to yourself, again and again, even when anxiety tries to pull you away.
