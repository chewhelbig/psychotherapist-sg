On OCD

OCD, Rituals, and What They're Trying to Manage

The ritual isn't the problem. It's the best solution the mind could find for something unbearable.

The hand-washing. The checking. The counting. The need for things to be exactly right — symmetrical, ordered, complete. The intrusive thought that won't stop circling.

From the outside, OCD looks irrational. From the inside, it makes perfect sense. It's the mind's attempt to manage terror.

What OCD Is Really Doing

OCD uses ritual to create a sense of control in a world that feels unsafe. The ritual says: "If I do this correctly, the bad thing won't happen. If I check one more time, I can be sure."

The relief is real — for a moment. Then the doubt returns, and the cycle starts again.

This is not weakness. It's a creative adjustment — a way the psyche found to manage overwhelming anxiety, often from very early in life.

What Lies Beneath the Ritual

In therapy, we don't just target the behaviour. We get curious about what the ritual is trying to manage. What's the fear? What would happen if you didn't check? What does "certainty" mean to you — and when did you first learn that the world wasn't safe?

Often, beneath OCD, we find a child who grew up in an unpredictable environment — where danger felt constant, where love was conditional, where something terrible happened and no one explained it.

How Therapy Helps

Gestalt therapy works with OCD not by fighting the ritual but by meeting the terror underneath it. As the underlying anxiety is acknowledged, expressed, and held in relationship, the grip of the ritual gradually loosens.

This doesn't happen overnight. OCD is deeply entrenched precisely because it was once necessary. The work is patient, relational, and respectful of the function the ritual served.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
