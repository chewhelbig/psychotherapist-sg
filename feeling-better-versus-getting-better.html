The Therapeutic Process

The Difference Between Feeling Better and Getting Better

Why therapy sometimes feels harder before it feels easier.

After a few therapy sessions, many people start to feel better. The relief of being heard, the novelty of having dedicated space to think, the sense that someone is paying attention — all of this produces a genuine lift.

And then, often around the fourth or fifth session, something shifts. The initial relief wears off. The deeper work begins.

This is the point where some people wonder if therapy is still "working."

It is. But feeling better and getting better aren't the same thing.

The Quick Fix Impulse

We live in a culture that rewards speed. Fix the problem. Optimise the solution. Move on.

Feeling better quickly can sometimes be a form of avoidance in disguise. You've offloaded the burden temporarily, but the underlying structure hasn't shifted. The same triggers will produce the same reactions. The same relational patterns will repeat.

I see this regularly in my practice. The person who arrives in crisis, feels relief after three sessions, and disappears — only to return a year later with the same pattern, slightly worse.

Why It Gets Harder

Genuine therapeutic change often involves a period of increased discomfort. This isn't a failure of the process. It's a feature of it.

When you start paying attention to things you've been avoiding — when you begin to feel emotions you've been suppressing, when you start challenging patterns that have "worked" (even if they've made you miserable) — the system pushes back.

The anxiety you feel when setting a new boundary. The grief that surfaces when you stop numbing. The anger that appears when you stop people-pleasing.

These are signs that something real is happening. The body that decided I am unworthy of love before the child had words cannot be argued out of its verdict by the adult who has acquired them. The work is slower and deeper than that.

What Lasting Change Looks Like

Getting better — in the deeper sense — is quieter than the initial relief. You notice that a situation which would have derailed you last year now feels manageable. You realise you've been sleeping through the night for weeks. A relationship that felt impossibly stuck begins to shift, not because the other person changed, but because you did.

The clients who get the most from therapy are not the ones who feel better fastest. They're the ones who stay with the process when it gets uncomfortable — who trust that the difficulty is part of the path, not a sign they're on the wrong one.

If you're in therapy and it's feeling harder than it did at the beginning, that might be exactly where you need to be.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
