On Psychotherapy

When Therapy Ends: How to Know You're Ready

Therapy has no fixed endpoint. But it does have an ending — and the ending matters.

People ask me this more often than you'd think: "How will I know when I'm done?"

The honest answer is that there's no graduation ceremony. No final exam. No therapist saying "you're cured."

Signs You Might Be Ready

You notice the issues that brought you to therapy no longer dominate your life. They may still be there — but they've moved from the foreground to the background.

You find yourself handling situations that once overwhelmed you with more steadiness. Not perfectly — but differently.

You catch yourself doing the work outside the room — noticing your patterns, pausing before reacting, making choices that feel more like yours.

You feel less urgency about the sessions. Not because you're avoiding — but because the ground feels more solid.

Why the Ending Matters

In psychotherapy, endings are not administrative. They are clinical. How we end matters — because many people have never had a relationship that ended well.

Relationships ended abruptly, without explanation. People disappeared. Conversations were avoided. Goodbyes were skipped.

The final session — or final few sessions — is an opportunity to do something different. To look back at what happened. To say what the work meant. To let the ending be conscious, mutual, and complete.

You Can Come Back

Ending therapy doesn't mean closing a door. Many clients return — months or years later — when life changes, when something surfaces, when they simply want a space to recalibrate.

Therapy is not a one-time fix. It's a resource. A relationship you can return to.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
