On Anxiety

Therapy for Anxiety: Beyond Coping Strategies

Why breathing exercises aren't enough when anxiety has roots in your history.

The internet is full of advice about anxiety. Breathe in for four, hold for seven, out for eight. Ground yourself. Splash cold water on your face.

These things work. In the moment.

But if you've been doing them for months — or years — and the anxiety keeps coming back, something deeper is asking for attention.

Coping Is Not Healing

Coping strategies manage symptoms. They regulate the nervous system temporarily.

But they don't ask why the nervous system is in overdrive in the first place.

Anxiety is not a malfunction. It's a signal. Coping techniques keep the signal quiet. Therapy listens to it.

What Lies Beneath

In my experience, anxiety rarely exists alone. Beneath it, you often find unprocessed grief, old shame, relationships where you learned it wasn't safe to need anything, perfectionism that was once a survival strategy, a body that learned very early to stay alert.

How Therapy Goes Deeper

In therapy, we don't just manage your anxiety. We get curious about it.

When did it start? What was happening in your life? What does it protect you from feeling? What happens when you let it be there without fighting it?

We track it in the body. We learn its language.

And slowly, the anxiety begins to loosen — not because you've suppressed it, but because the thing underneath it has been seen and met.

The Difference You'll Notice

Coping looks like: I can manage my anxiety when it comes.

Therapy looks like: I understand why it's there, and it doesn't run my life anymore.

If you're considering therapy — the first step is to come.
