On Psychotherapy

Gestalt Therapy: What It Is and How It's Different

Not a technique — a way of being with another person.

Most people in Singapore have heard of CBT. Some know about psychoanalysis. Gestalt therapy is less familiar — and harder to explain in a sentence, because it's not a technique. It's a way of being with another person.

The Word Itself

Gestalt is German. It means something like "form" or "whole pattern." The word points to how we organise experience — what stands out, what recedes, what we notice and what we avoid noticing.

What Makes It Different

CBT works with thoughts. It asks: what are you thinking, and is that thought accurate?

Gestalt therapy doesn't start with thoughts. It starts with awareness. What are you experiencing right now — in your body, your emotions, the space between us?

The change doesn't come from correcting anything. It comes from seeing clearly what is actually happening — and staying with it long enough for something to shift on its own.

This is what we call the paradoxical theory of change: change happens not by trying to be different, but by fully becoming what you already are.

In Practice

A Gestalt session doesn't follow a protocol. There's no worksheet, no homework assignment.

Instead, I'm tracking what's happening in the room. Where your energy goes. When you deflect. When your voice drops. What your body does when you talk about something painful.

Sometimes I'll invite you to try something — say a sentence out loud, stay with a feeling for a moment longer, notice what happens in your chest when you think about your father. These are ways of bringing something into sharper focus so you can actually see it.

The Relationship

Gestalt therapy is deeply relational. The therapeutic relationship is not a backdrop to the work — it is the work.

How you are with me tells us something about how you are in the world. And how I respond — with presence, honesty, and care — becomes a new relational experience.

Who It's For

Anyone. It's particularly effective for people who have done a lot of thinking about their problems but haven't been able to feel their way through them. If you understand your patterns intellectually but keep repeating them, Gestalt therapy may reach something that cognitive approaches don't.

If you're considering therapy — the first step is to come.
