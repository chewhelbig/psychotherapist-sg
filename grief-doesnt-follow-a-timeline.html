Grief & Loss

Grief Doesn't Follow a Timeline

There is no right pace. There is only yours.

Almost everyone who has experienced significant loss has encountered the "stages of grief" — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance. The model is so culturally embedded that people use it to measure their own progress: "I think I'm in the bargaining stage." "I should be at acceptance by now."

As though grief were a staircase you climb, one orderly step at a time.

But grief doesn't work that way. It never did.

Grief Is Not Linear

Real grief comes in waves — sometimes predictable (anniversaries, holidays, a song), sometimes out of nowhere. A stranger's laugh that sounds like theirs. The smell of a particular soap. You can be fine for weeks and then be flattened by a Tuesday afternoon for no apparent reason.

This isn't regression. It's how grief actually moves through us: not in stages but in spirals — circling back to the same emotions at different depths, each time with a slightly different quality.

The Grief That Doesn't Look Like Grief

We tend to recognise grief when it follows a death. But many of the losses people carry into my consulting room don't fit that category.

The end of a relationship. A career that didn't materialise. An estrangement from family. A version of yourself you had to leave behind. A country you left and can't return to in the same way.

These losses are real. But because they're harder to name, people carry them alone, wondering why they can't simply "move on." The culture isn't comfortable with grief that lingers. After a socially acceptable period, the expectation is that you'll be "back to normal." The support that was abundant at first quietly withdraws.

But grief doesn't follow social timelines any more than it follows clinical ones.

What Therapy Offers

Therapy doesn't speed grief up. It doesn't offer a shortcut to acceptance.

What it offers is company. Consistent, unhurried, non-judgmental company for a process that our culture often expects you to do alone and quickly.

In my practice, we sit with grief as it is — not as it should be. We pay attention to what the loss has stirred in you, what it has revealed about what matters, what it has disrupted in your sense of self and your sense of the world.

If you're grieving and it's taking longer than you think it should, consider the possibility that you're not doing it wrong. You're doing it at the pace your particular loss requires.

Some losses take years to integrate. Some become part of who you are — not because you haven't healed, but because the love that produced the grief doesn't disappear.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
