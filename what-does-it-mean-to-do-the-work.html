The Therapeutic Process

What Does It Mean to 'Do the Work' in Therapy?

It doesn't look like what you think.

"Doing the work" has become one of those phrases that floats through self-help culture without much explanation. People say it knowingly, as though it's obvious. But when you're sitting in my waiting room before your fourth session, you might reasonably wonder: what is this "work," exactly?

The Difference Between Thinking and Experiencing

Many intelligent, reflective people have spent years thinking about their issues. They can articulate exactly what went wrong in their childhood, precisely why they choose unavailable partners, and specifically how their anxiety operates.

And yet nothing changes.

That's because understanding and experiencing are different things. I see this with such regularity that I no longer consider it the exception. A person sits across from me who has read the books, done the worksheets, learned to identify their triggers, developed self-compassion techniques. They can articulate their patterns with impressive precision.

And they are still suffering.

The work was not wrong. But the suffering was never cognitive. It was somatic, preverbal, encoded in the nervous system before the brain had developed the capacity for the thoughts the reframing is designed to change. The affirmations float above the wound like oil on water — present, visible, unable to penetrate the surface.

What It Actually Looks Like

Therapeutic work happens at the level of experience — in the body, in the relationship with the therapist, in the present moment.

Some sessions are quiet. You arrive not knowing what to talk about, and something unexpected surfaces. Other sessions are intense — you touch something that's been buried for a long time, and it takes energy to stay present with it.

The breath that catches mid-sentence. The hand that instinctively goes to the throat. The flush that no amount of therapeutic fluency can prevent, because the flush is not cognitive. It is the autonomic nervous system announcing what the conscious mind has been narrating around.

This is the work: staying present when the body interrupts the mind's performance. Feeling what you've been describing. Stepping off the observation platform and onto the ground.

Between Sessions

The work continues in small ways. You catch yourself reacting differently to a situation that would have previously triggered you. You notice a thought pattern you hadn't seen before. You make a choice you wouldn't have made six months ago — not because you forced yourself, but because something shifted.

It's Not Always Comfortable

Real work often involves discomfort. Setting a boundary you've never set. Expressing anger you've always swallowed. Sitting with sadness instead of distracting yourself from it.

The therapist's role is to make this process safe enough to attempt — not comfortable, but safe. There's an important difference.

How You Know It's Working

You won't always feel like therapy is "working" while it's happening. Progress isn't linear. But over time: you sleep better. Your relationships feel less exhausting. You make decisions with more clarity. You react less and respond more.

Therapy isn't something that happens to you here. It unfolds with you.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
