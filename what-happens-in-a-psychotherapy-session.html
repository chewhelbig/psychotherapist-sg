On Psychotherapy

What Happens in a Psychotherapy Session

The room, the beginning, the silence between words — what it actually feels like.

The Room

It's quieter than you'd expect. Two chairs. Sometimes my dog Mulan is there, curled up and unbothered. No clipboard, no diagnostic checklist, no scripted questions.

You sit down. I sit across from you. And we begin.

The Beginning

Sometimes people know exactly why they're here. They've rehearsed it in the car. The words come quickly — a story, a crisis, a question they need answered.

Other times, people sit down and don't quite know where to start. They trail off. They say "I don't even know why I'm here." That's fine. That's often where the most important work begins.

I'm not waiting for you to perform your distress correctly. I'm paying attention to what's happening — in your body, your voice, the silences between your words.

What I'm Doing

I'm not analysing you. I'm not sitting there with a theory about what's wrong with you.

I'm noticing. What shifts when you talk about your mother. Where your breath catches. The moment you look away. The thing you almost said but didn't.

Sometimes I'll name what I see. Sometimes I'll ask you to stay with something a little longer — a feeling, a sensation, a memory that surfaced. Not to push. To let it unfold at a pace that feels safe.

It's Not Advice

People sometimes expect therapy to feel like consulting an expert who will tell you what to do. Fix the marriage. Quit the job. Set the boundary.

That's not what happens here.

Therapy is more like sitting with someone who helps you hear what you already know — but haven't yet been able to say out loud, or feel fully, or act on.

What It Feels Like

Some sessions feel like relief. You say the thing you've been carrying, and the room holds it.

Some sessions feel uncomfortable. You touch something raw and unfinished. That discomfort is not a sign that something went wrong. It's usually a sign that something real is surfacing.

Some sessions are quiet. Not every moment needs words.

After

You might feel lighter. Or heavier. Or strangely clear. Or confused in a new way.

All of that is the work doing what it does.

Therapy isn't something that happens to you here. It unfolds with you.

If you're considering therapy — the first step is to come.
