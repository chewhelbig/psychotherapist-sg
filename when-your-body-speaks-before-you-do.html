On the Body

When Your Body Speaks Before You Do

The body keeps what the mind refuses. Therapy listens to both.

You tell me you're fine. But your jaw is clenched. Your shoulders are at your ears. Your breath is shallow and fast.

The body doesn't lie. It can't. It doesn't have the luxury of rationalisation.

What the Body Holds

In my practice, I see this daily. The executive whose back seized the week before a promotion. The woman whose eczema flares every time she visits her parents. The man whose stomach knots whenever his partner raises a concern.

These are not random. The body is speaking — clearly, consistently — about something the mind hasn't yet been able to name.

Why Gestalt Therapy Works with the Body

Gestalt therapy has always understood that the person is not a mind sitting on top of a body. We are one system. Emotion lives in the muscles, the breath, the gut, the skin. When we ignore the body, we miss half of what's happening.

In a session, I might notice that your voice drops when you talk about your father. Or that you hold your breath when you approach something painful. Or that your hands grip the chair when you say "I'm not angry."

I don't interpret these. I invite you to notice them. "What's happening in your chest right now?" "Can you stay with that sensation for a moment?"

This Isn't Bodywork

I'm not doing massage or breathwork. I'm not asking you to move in specific ways. I'm simply paying attention to the body as it communicates in real time — and inviting you to listen to yourself with the same attention.

When people begin to notice their body's signals, something shifts. The anxiety that felt overwhelming becomes a tightness in the chest that can be breathed through. The numbness reveals a sadness that was waiting to be felt.

The Body Remembers

Trauma, in particular, lives in the body long after the mind has filed it away. You may not consciously remember the event, but your nervous system does. It responds to triggers with the same intensity it did originally — racing heart, frozen muscles, the urge to flee.

Working with the body in therapy allows these stored responses to be met, understood, and gradually released.

If something here resonated — therapy is a place to explore it further.
