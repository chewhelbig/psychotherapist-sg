On Trauma

What Trauma Therapy Looks Like (Gestalt + EMDR)

For people who suspect they have trauma but are scared of the process.

The word trauma can feel too big. People often say: "I wasn't in a war. I wasn't abused. What I went through doesn't count."

It counts.

Trauma is not defined by the event. It's defined by what the experience did to you — to your nervous system, your sense of safety, your ability to trust yourself and others.

What People Fear About Trauma Therapy

That they'll be forced to relive the worst moments. That it will break them open and there will be no putting themselves back together.

These fears make sense. But trauma therapy — done well — does not re-traumatise. It moves carefully, at your pace, within a relationship where you feel safe enough to approach what has been avoided.

Gestalt Therapy and Trauma

Gestalt therapy works with trauma in the present tense. We don't just talk about what happened. We notice what's happening now — in your body, your emotions, the space between us — as the past surfaces.

Trauma fragments experience. Gestalt therapy gently brings these fragments into awareness so they can be integrated — not re-lived, but made whole.

EMDR

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation — usually guided eye movements — to help the brain process traumatic memories that have become "stuck."

It can feel strange at first. But many people find it remarkably effective — sometimes shifting something in a session or two that talk therapy alone hadn't reached in months.

What the Process Looks Like

We don't start with the trauma. We start with safety. Building trust. Establishing resources.

Only when you feel ready do we approach the traumatic material. And "ready" is your call, not mine.

What Changes

The memories don't disappear. But they lose their charge. The thing that used to flood you with panic or shame becomes something you can hold — something that happened, but no longer defines you.

Sleep improves. The body softens. Relationships change. You begin to trust yourself again.

If you're considering therapy — the first step is to come.
